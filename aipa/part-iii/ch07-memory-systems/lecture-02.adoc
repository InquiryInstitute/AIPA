=== 7.2: Vector Stores and Similarity Search
:leveloffset: 2

Imagine a personal assistant that can, in the time it takes you to blink, pull the single paragraph from a ten‑million‑page legal archive that answers a user’s question *exactly*. The assistant must decide *which* paragraph to return, and it must do so before the user can finish typing. The tension between “fast enough” and “right enough” is the heart of vector stores.

[.epigraph]
"Similarity is not identity—but it is often enough."
— Reimers & Gurevych, *Sentence‑BERT* <<Reimers2019>>

==== Example Prompts

* “Find documents similar to this query.” — _Embed query, search vector store, return top‑k_  
* “Index our knowledge base.” — _Documents → chunks → embed → index_  
* “What’s most relevant to this question?” — _Similarity search; retrieval quality matters_  

==== Conceptual Core

When Jane asked the assistant for the *force majeure* clause in a contract, the system returned a paragraph about “termination for convenience”. The answer was *semantically* close—both discuss ways a contract can end—but it was *factually* wrong. The failure illustrates the central paradox of vector stores: **semantic proximity is a useful proxy for relevance, but it is not a guarantee**. The system’s mistake was not a bug in the search algorithm; it was a mismatch between the geometry of the embedding space and the legal nuance of the query.

A *vector store* is simply an index that holds dense vectors (embeddings) and can retrieve the *k* vectors nearest to a query vector. An *embedding* is a learned function that maps any piece of text (or image, audio, etc.) into a point in ℝ^d such that inputs with similar meaning land close together. The store therefore turns the problem of “find the right paragraph” into “find the nearest points in a high‑dimensional space”. This idea dates back to early nearest‑neighbor experiments in the 1990s, but the explosion of large‑language‑model (LLM) encoders has made it viable at web‑scale.

Dense vectors typically have 384, 768, or 1 536 dimensions. Similarity is measured with cosine similarity (the angle between vectors) or the dot product (the length‑scaled projection). Given a query embedding **q** and a stored vector **vᵢ**, we seek  

\[
\operatorname*{arg\,min}_{i\in\mathcal{D}} \; \text{dist}(q, v_i)
\]

where *dist* is 1 – cosine or –dot. A naïve exact search examines every vector, an **O(n)** operation that becomes untenable for millions of chunks. Approximate nearest‑neighbor (ANN) structures—most notably **HNSW** (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) and **IVF** (Inverted File)—replace the linear scan with a graph or quantisation that can be traversed in sub‑linear time. In a corpus of one million chunks, exact search returns the true nearest neighbor 99.8 % of the time in ~800 ms, while HNSW returns it 97 % of the time in ~10 ms. For most Retrieval‑Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines, that few‑percent recall loss is acceptable because the downstream language model can compensate with context.

The bridge between raw documents and vectors is **chunking**. Documents are split into overlapping fragments—usually 256–512 tokens with a 50‑token overlap—before embedding. Chunk size is a design knob: too small and the fragment loses the surrounding context needed for a meaningful embedding; too large and the embedding averages over unrelated material, diluting relevance. Empirical studies show that *semantic chunking* (splitting at sentence or paragraph boundaries) plus modest overlap yields the best trade‑off for legal texts, scientific articles, and conversational logs alike.

Finally, similarity is never neutral. Embeddings inherit the distributional biases of the corpora on which they were trained. If the training data over‑represents corporate law and under‑represents indigenous legal traditions, the vector store will systematically surface corporate clauses while pushing community‑generated clauses to the periphery. In effect, the store becomes a *political filter* that shapes what the assistant “knows”.

===== Key Points

* Semantic proximity ≠ factual relevance; it is a *proxy* that can fail on nuanced queries.  
* Vector store = dense‑vector index + similarity search (cosine or dot).  
* ANN structures (HNSW, IVF) give sub‑linear query time at a modest recall cost.  
* Chunk size & overlap are critical engineering levers for retrieval quality.  
* Indexing pipeline: document → semantic chunks → embed → store.  
* Query pipeline: embed query → ANN search → top‑k chunks.  
* Embedding bias → retrieval bias → epistemic bias in downstream agents.  
* Designing a vector store is both a technical and a normative act.  

==== Technical Example & Live Demo

*Demo 1 (3 min):* A short screencast shows a query “force majeure clause” being embedded, the HNSW graph traversal visualised as a series of expanding circles, and the top‑5 chunks returned with their similarity scores. The demo highlights three moments where the algorithm prunes a neighbor because its cosine similarity falls below the current threshold.

**Step 1 – Index Construction (≈ 200 words)**  
Take a corpus of 10 000 legal contracts. Each contract is split into 512‑token chunks with a 50‑token overlap. Using a Sentence‑BERT encoder (`llm.embed`), each chunk is turned into a 768‑dimensional vector. The vectors are stored in a Chroma backend configured with an HNSW index (`M=32`, `efConstruction=200`). The resulting index occupies ~2 GB of RAM and can be persisted to disk for later reuse.

**Step 2 – Query Execution (≈ 250 words)**  
When a user asks “What is a force majeure clause?”, the query text is first embedded with the same Sentence‑BERT model, producing **q**. The HNSW index starts from a randomly chosen entry point and climbs the hierarchy layer by layer, examining only the neighbors whose cosine similarity exceeds the current best score. After descending to the bottom layer, the algorithm returns the five highest‑scoring chunks. In our 10 k‑chunk store, the whole operation completes in ~12 ms on a modest laptop CPU. The returned chunks include the correct clause, a surrounding paragraph, and two “near‑miss” clauses about “termination for convenience”, illustrating the similarity‑vs‑identity tension.

**Step 3 – Evaluation & Tuning (≈ 150 words)**  
We evaluate *recall@5* on a held‑out set of 200 manually annotated queries. Baseline HNSW (`efSearch=40`) yields 88 % recall; raising `efSearch` to 80 pushes recall to 93 % at the cost of ~18 ms latency. If latency must stay below 20 ms, we settle on `efSearch=60`, achieving a balanced 90 % recall. For domains where rare terminology is critical (e.g., tribal land‑rights clauses), we supplement the dense store with a sparse lexical index and perform a hybrid merge at rank‑time.

===== Key Points

* Indexing: chunk → embed → HNSW (M=32, efConstruction=200).  
* Query: embed → ANN traversal → top‑k (≈ 12 ms for 10 k vectors).  
* Recall@k vs. latency trade‑off controlled by `efSearch`.  
* Hybrid retrieval (dense + sparse) rescues rare terms.  
* Demo 1 visualises the graph traversal and similarity pruning.  

==== Philosophical Reflection

Retrieval is an act of *memory access* that relies on a *proxy*: vectors that are “close” in a mathematical sense. Two chunks can be mathematically close while being conceptually unrelated—a phenomenon that caused Jane’s erroneous answer. The proxy works well when the embedding space faithfully captures the domain’s conceptual structure, but it breaks down for rare terminology, emerging legal concepts, or culturally specific phrasing that the encoder never saw during training.

The politics of similarity ask: *who decides what counts as “close”?* Because embeddings are trained on large, publicly available corpora, they inherit the dominant cultural and linguistic patterns of those sources. If a corpus over‑represents corporate law and under‑represents indigenous legal traditions, the vector store will preferentially surface corporate clauses, marginalising other perspectives. Retrieval therefore becomes a *political act* that shapes the epistemic landscape of any downstream agent.

**Mitigation strategies (≈ 120 words)**  
1. **Curated augmentation** – deliberately inject under‑represented texts (e.g., community‑generated statutes) into the store.  
2. **Debiasing transforms** – identify gender or racial subspaces in the embedding and project vectors away from them before indexing.  
3. **Periodic audits** – run a battery of queries that span demographic slices and compare recall rates; flag systematic gaps.  
4. **Hybrid retrieval** – combine dense similarity with classic lexical matching (BM25) to rescue rare terms that dense vectors miss.  
5. **Human‑in‑the‑loop re‑ranking** – let domain experts review top‑k results and provide feedback that can be used to fine‑tune the encoder or adjust index parameters.

By treating the vector store as a *design artifact* rather than a neutral data structure, we can steer the memory system toward a more inclusive epistemic horizon, even while accepting that similarity will never be a perfect stand‑in for relevance.

===== Key Points

* Retrieval returns “similar” vectors, not the *true* answer.  
* Embedding bias → retrieval bias → epistemic bias in downstream agents.  
* Dominant training data shapes what is considered “similar”.  
* Failure modes appear for rare, domain‑specific, or culturally specific language.  
* Mitigation: augment, debias, audit, hybrid retrieval, human feedback.  
* Designing a vector store is both an engineering and a normative decision.
