==== Hook: The Spike That Stopped the Chat

It’s 9 a.m. on a weekday, and a breaking‑news alert pushes a sudden five‑fold surge of users onto your customer‑support chatbot. Within seconds the average *time‑to‑first‑token* (TTFT) jumps from 0.7 s to 12 s, the UI freezes, and the support queue backs up. Users start posting on social media: “The bot is broken!” The incident forces the team to ask, **“How do we keep the service usable when traffic explodes?”** This concrete crisis will drive today’s exploration of throughput, latency, and the engineering choices that turn a fragile demo into a production‑grade orchestrator.

==== Conceptual Core

When a system that was previously humming at 200 req/s suddenly receives 1 000 req/s, the first question is not “*Can we buy more GPUs?*” but “*What is actually breaking, and how do we measure it?*” The answer begins with a **service‑level objective (SLO)** that translates business expectations into hard numbers. For a chat‑assistant we typically track the 95th‑percentile latency (p95) because the long tail is what users notice, and we track sustained throughput (requests per second, rps) to ensure the back‑end can keep up. A realistic SLO might read: *p95 ≤ 3 s and ≥ 50 req/s under normal load; under peak load we must still guarantee p95 ≤ 5 s at 200 req/s.* By committing to these numbers early, every scaling decision can be judged against a concrete north star.

**Profiling the pipeline** turns the vague feeling of “it’s slow” into a data‑driven story. An LLM‑driven orchestrator can be broken into three logical stages: (a) prompt preparation (tokenisation, context stitching), (b) model inference (GPU kernel launch, attention computation), and (c) tool orchestration (API calls, database look‑ups, result aggregation). By instrumenting each stage with high‑resolution timestamps—e.g., `t_prompt_start`, `t_prompt_end`, `t_llm_start`, …—we obtain a per‑request latency breakdown. Plotting these breakdowns across many requests yields a histogram that often reveals a **long tail**: a small fraction of requests spend a disproportionate amount of time waiting in a queue. Queueing theory tells us that the utilisation factor ρ = λ/μ (arrival rate over service rate) drives this tail; as ρ approaches 1, the expected waiting time grows roughly as 1/(1‑ρ). In the 9 a.m. spike, we observed λ ≈ 900 req/s while the LLM service rate μ was only 800 req/s, giving ρ ≈ 1.125 and explaining the dramatic latency explosion.

Once the bottleneck is identified, the team must choose a mitigation path. The **Decision‑Making Matrix** is a living artifact that maps the identified bottleneck to three families of techniques—caching, parallelism, and batching—while also surfacing cost, complexity, and expected latency impact. For the chatbot spike the matrix looked like this:

| Bottleneck | Caching | Parallelism | Batching |
|------------|---------|------------|----------|
| LLM inference (compute‑bound) | ✅ Hash‑based cache of repeat prompts (≈ 30 % hit‑rate) | ❌ Limited gain; inference is largely single‑threaded | ✅ Group 5‑10 prompts per GPU kernel (≈ 40 % latency reduction) |
| External tool (I/O‑bound) | ✅ Cache deterministic API responses (TTL = 300 s) | ✅ Issue up to N calls concurrently (bounded by downstream rate limits) | ❌ Batching often adds extra round‑trip latency |
| Network latency | ✅ Edge CDN cache for static assets | ✅ Parallel TCP streams reduce wall‑clock | ✅ Combine calls when APIs support bulk endpoints |

Each cell is annotated with a rough *cost* (CPU/GPU, memory, engineering effort) and an *expected Δ ms* impact. The matrix forces the team to ask, “If we cache, how often will the same prompt appear? If we parallelise, can downstream services handle the extra concurrency?” In our case study the first mitigation was to enable a Redis cache keyed by a SHA‑256 hash of the full prompt plus tool inputs. The cache hit‑rate rose to 78 % after the first minute of the spike, immediately pulling p95 down to ~4 s. The remaining tail was traced to a slow third‑party pricing API, prompting the second mitigation: parallelising those calls with an `asyncio.gather` pattern limited to 20 concurrent connections. This shaved another 0.7 s off p95.

**Deep dive into the three families** clarifies why they are not interchangeable. *Caching* is a stateful optimisation; it requires a deterministic mapping from request to response, a TTL policy that balances staleness against cache size, and an invalidation strategy when the model version changes. *Parallelism* is a concurrency technique that shines when the bottleneck is I/O‑bound; it demands an async runtime, back‑pressure handling, and careful budgeting of open sockets to avoid “thundering herd” failures. *Batching* aggregates independent work units into a single compute call, amortising GPU kernel launch overhead and improving utilisation, but it introduces queuing delay that must be modelled with a *batch‑size vs. latency* curve. Empirically, we found that batch sizes of 5‑8 prompts gave the best trade‑off for our A100‑based inference service, while larger batches caused p95 to creep upward because the queue waited too long for enough requests to fill.

Finally, **infrastructure limits and sustainability** remind us that scaling is bounded by physics, economics, and ethics. Adding another A100 instance raises power draw by ≈ 250 W and, depending on the data‑center’s energy mix, adds ≈ 0.5 kg CO₂ per hour. Cost scales linearly with instance count, yet latency improvements follow a diminishing‑returns curve because of Amdahl’s law and the overhead of coordination. By documenting *maximum sustainable throughput* (e.g., 300 req/s on a single‑region deployment) and *energy per inference* (≈ 0.5 g CO₂), teams can present a transparent trade‑off matrix to product owners and sustainability officers. This turns a technical metric into a governance artifact that can be audited, reported, and, if necessary, constrained by policy.

===== Key Points

* Define concrete SLOs (p95 latency, sustained rps) before any scaling work.  
* Instrument each pipeline stage (prompt, LLM, tools) to obtain a per‑component latency breakdown.  
* Use utilisation ρ = λ/μ and queueing theory to predict when a tail will appear.  
* Populate a decision‑making matrix that maps the identified bottleneck to caching, parallelism, or batching, with cost and latency impact estimates.  
* Caching: hash‑based keys, TTL policy, and invalidation on model version change; monitor hit‑ratio.  
* Parallelism: async runtimes, bounded concurrency, back‑pressure handling for I/O‑bound tools.  
* Batching: group 5‑8 prompts per GPU call, plot batch‑size vs. latency curve, watch queuing delay.  
* Record energy per inference and cost per request; expose these as governance artifacts.  
* Iterate: re‑profile after each mitigation, compare p95 and throughput, and adjust the matrix.  

==== Technical Example

We will walk through a reproducible load‑test using Python’s `asyncio` and the open‑source tool *locust*. The example demonstrates profiling, applying a mitigation, and measuring the impact.

[source,python]
----
import asyncio, aiohttp, time, hashlib, json
from statistics import mean
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

ENDPOINT = "https://orchestrator.example.com/chat"

async def call_orchestrator(prompt):
    async with aiohttp.ClientSession() as session:
        start = time.time()
        async with session.post(ENDPOINT, json={"prompt": prompt}) as resp:
            await resp.json()
        latency = time.time() - start
        return latency

async def load_test(prompts, concurrency):
    sem = asyncio.Semaphore(concurrency)
    async def worker(p):
        async with sem:
            return await call_orchestrator(p)
    tasks = [worker(p) for p in prompts]
    latencies = await asyncio.gather(*tasks)
    return latencies

if __name__ == "__main__":
    prompts = ["Explain quantum computing"] * 200   # repeat to expose cache
    for conc in (10, 50, 100):
        lat = asyncio.run(load_test(prompts, conc))
        p95 = sorted(lat)[int(0.95*len(lat))-1]
        print(f"Concurrency {conc}: p95={p95:.2f}s, avg={mean(lat):.2f}s")
        # quick histogram for visual feedback
        plt.hist(lat, bins=20, alpha=0.6)
        plt.title(f"Latency distribution @ concurrency {conc}")
        plt.xlabel("seconds")
        plt.ylabel("requests")
        plt.show()
----
*Baseline run (no cache):* p95 ≈ 4.8 s at 10 cps, spikes to 12 s at 100 cps.  
*After adding Redis cache (hash of prompt as key, TTL = 300 s):* cache‑hit ratio ≈ 78 %; p95 drops to 2.1 s at 100 cps.  
*Parallel tool calls:* By converting sequential API calls inside the orchestrator to `asyncio.gather`, the tool‑stage latency fell from 1.2 s to 0.4 s, shaving overall p95 by ≈ 0.7 s.  
*Batching LLM inference:* Grouping 5 prompts per GPU request reduced GPU kernel overhead from 0.9 s to 0.3 s per request, further improving p95 to 1.6 s at 200 req/s.

**Instrumentation checklist**

* Log timestamps for *prompt‑prep*, *LLM‑call*, *each tool call*, and *response assembly*.  
* Export per‑component latency to a Prometheus gauge; visualize in Grafana.  
* Record CPU/GPU utilisation and network I/O alongside request metrics.  
* Track cache‑hit ratio and batch‑size distribution.  
* Plot latency histograms before and after each mitigation to see the tail shrink.

After each mitigation, re‑run the load test and compare the latency histogram and throughput curve. The iterative process mirrors a real‑world performance‑tuning sprint.

===== Key Points

* Use async load‑testing (`asyncio`, `locust`) to generate concurrent traffic.  
* Capture per‑component latency (prompt, LLM, each tool) for bottleneck identification.  
* Implement a hash‑based Redis cache; monitor hit ratio and TTL.  
* Parallelise I/O‑bound tool calls with bounded concurrency.  
* Batch multiple prompts into a single GPU inference to amortise kernel launch cost.  
* Re‑measure p95 latency and throughput after each change; document the delta.  

==== Philosophical Reflection

**Scale as a governance artifact.**  
The NIST AI RMF stresses *Resource Management* as a core function: “ensure that computational resources are allocated, monitored, and retired responsibly.” When we document *maximum sustainable throughput* and *energy per inference*, we turn an engineering metric into a governance artifact that can be audited, reported, and aligned with corporate sustainability goals.

**Stakeholder responsibility matrix.**  

| Stakeholder | Primary Concern | Decision Authority |
|-------------|----------------|--------------------|
| Developers | Code correctness, latency optimisations | Choose caching keys, parallelism patterns |
| Product owners | User experience, SLA compliance | Set SLO thresholds, approve cost trade‑offs |
| Operations (SRE) | Infrastructure cost, reliability | Size instances, configure autoscaling policies |
| End‑users | Responsiveness, availability | Indirect – experience informs SLOs |
| Sustainability officers | Energy use, carbon footprint | Approve scaling limits, enforce green‑cloud policies |

Explicitly mapping who owns each scaling decision prevents “responsibility gaps” that can lead to over‑provisioning or hidden technical debt.

**Environmental and economic externalities.**  
A single inference on a modern GPU can emit ≈ 0.5 g CO₂. Scaling to 10 k req/s therefore adds ≈ 5 kg CO₂ per hour—a non‑trivial footprint. By quantifying this cost, teams can justify investments in more efficient models (e.g., quantised LLMs) or in renewable‑energy‑backed cloud regions. The philosophical takeaway: *scaling is a moral choice as much as a technical one*.
