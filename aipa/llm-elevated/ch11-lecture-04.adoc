==== Hook: A Real‑World Dilemma

Your product team has just finished a pilot of a new conversational‑agent configuration—*Chatbot‑X v2*—that promises **2 % higher user‑satisfaction** and **10 % lower latency**. After a week of limited rollout, the support desk is split: half of the agents are already using v2, while the other half remain on the legacy v1. Management asks, *“Do we ship this to all users now, or should we wait for more evidence?”* The tension is palpable: a faster, happier chatbot could win market share, but an unnoticed regression (e.g., a subtle bias in response style) could damage brand trust. The answer hinges on a disciplined, statistical comparison—not on a handful of anecdotal screenshots or a single success story. In the next ninety minutes we will turn this dilemma into a rigorous A/B experiment, interpret the results responsibly, and embed the practice into AI governance.

==== Conceptual Core

*Paragraph 1 – Framing the Counterfactual*  
The core question is not “Did we see a better number?” but “Would the same users have experienced that outcome if we had **not** changed the system?” A controlled comparison creates the counterfactual by allocating traffic to a **control arm** (Chatbot‑X v1) and a **treatment arm** (Chatbot‑X v2). Random assignment—whether per request, per user session, or per geographic region—ensures that, on average, the two arms are balanced on all observed and unobserved characteristics (device type, time of day, user expertise). This balance is the statistical foundation that lets us attribute any systematic difference in outcomes to the treatment rather than to confounding factors.

*Paragraph 2 – Crafting a Testable Hypothesis*  
A well‑designed A/B test begins with a **pre‑registered hypothesis** that translates a business goal into a statistical statement. For Chatbot‑X we might write: *“Version 2 reduces average response latency by at least 5 % while increasing user‑satisfaction scores by at least 1 %.”* The hypothesis defines **primary metrics** (latency, satisfaction) and **secondary metrics** (token cost, fairness score). Pre‑registration—recorded in a shared experiment registry—locks the hypothesis, metric definitions, and analysis plan before any data are seen, protecting against post‑hoc rationalisation.

*Paragraph 3 – Sample‑Size Planning and Power*  
Before launching, we must ask: “How many requests do we need to detect the promised effect with high confidence?” A **power analysis** uses the expected effect size, the desired statistical power (commonly 80 % or 90 %), and the acceptable Type I error rate (α = 0.05). For latency, a 5 % reduction translates to roughly 15 ms on a baseline of 300 ms. Assuming a standard deviation of 60 ms, a two‑sided t‑test with 80 % power requires about 45 000 requests per arm. For satisfaction, a 1 % lift on a 85 % baseline with a standard deviation of 12 % needs roughly 70 000 requests per arm. The larger of the two determines the experiment duration. Documenting these calculations in the experiment plan is a best‑practice requirement.

*Paragraph 4 – Inference, Confidence, and Effect Size*  
After data collection, we compute **test statistics** (difference of means for latency, difference of proportions for satisfaction) and evaluate them under the **null hypothesis** of no effect. The **p‑value** tells us how surprising the observed statistic would be if the null were true; a conventional threshold α = 0.05 flags results that are unlikely to be due to chance. More informative are **confidence intervals (CIs)**, which give a plausible range for the true effect (e.g., “latency improves by 8 ms ± 3 ms, 95 % CI”). **Effect size** (Cohen’s d for latency, risk‑ratio for satisfaction) quantifies practical significance independent of sample size, allowing decision‑makers to weigh the benefit against deployment cost.

*Paragraph 5 – Multiple Comparisons, Interim Looks, and Early Stopping*  
Real‑world experiments rarely involve a single metric. If we test latency, satisfaction, cost, and a fairness score simultaneously, the probability of at least one false positive inflates. **Bonferroni correction** (α / k) is conservative; **Benjamini‑Hochberg** controls the false‑discovery rate more powerfully. Moreover, many teams peek at interim results to accelerate decisions. **Sequential analysis** (e.g., O’Brien‑Fleming boundaries) or **group‑sequential designs** allow early stopping for efficacy or futility while preserving the overall Type I error rate. Any interim look must be pre‑specified in the analysis plan, and the stopping rule must be applied automatically by the experiment platform.

*Paragraph 6 – Checklist for a Governance‑Ready A/B Test*  

1. **Pre‑register** hypothesis, primary/secondary metrics, sample‑size calculations, and interim‑analysis plan.  
2. **Randomize** traffic at the granularity that matches the causal question (request, session, or region).  
3. **Collect** all metrics uniformly, tagging each observation with arm, timestamp, and context variables.  
4. **Perform** power analysis upfront; verify that the observed sample meets the planned size before unblinding.  
5. **Compute** p‑value, 95 % CI, and effect size for each metric; apply the chosen multiple‑comparison correction.  
6. **Implement** sequential monitoring if early‑stopping is desired; log every interim peek.  
7. **Report** a complete results table (Δ, CI, p‑value, correction method) and visualisations with error bars.  
8. **Interpret** statistical findings in the context of business impact, equity considerations, and regulatory thresholds.  
9. **Archive** the full experiment artefacts (code, data schema, analysis notebook) for auditability.  

===== Key Points

* Counterfactual reasoning via randomized control vs. treatment arms  
* Pre‑registration of hypothesis, metrics, and analysis plan  
* Power analysis & sample‑size justification for latency & satisfaction  
* Test statistics, p‑value, confidence interval, and effect size  
* Multiple‑comparison corrections (Bonferroni, Benjamini‑Hochberg)  
* Sequential analysis and early‑stopping rules  
* Transparent reporting (tables, error‑bar charts)  
* Governance checklist linking statistical rigor to auditability  

==== Technical Example

*Paragraph 1 – Experiment Set‑Up for Chatbot‑X*  
We implement a 50 / 50 traffic split between **Prompt A** (baseline) and **Prompt B** (new template). Over a 72‑hour window we collect 80 000 requests per arm, exceeding the power‑analysis requirement for both latency and satisfaction. Each request is logged with the schema:

```
| request_id | arm | latency_ms | satisfaction_score | cost_usd | fairness_flag |
|------------|-----|------------|--------------------|----------|----------------|
| 000001     | A   | 312        | 0.84               | 0.0045   | 0              |
| 000002     | B   | 298        | 0.86               | 0.0042   | 1              |
| …          | …   | …          | …                  | …        | …              |
```

The `fairness_flag` is a binary indicator derived from a downstream bias detector (1 = potentially unfair response). All fields are collected automatically by the governance simulator, which timestamps each row and stores the randomisation seed for reproducibility.

*Paragraph 2 – Power Confirmation and Interim Monitoring*  
Before unblinding, we confirm that the observed sample size meets the planned 45 000‑request threshold for latency and 70 000 for satisfaction. Using the `statsmodels` library we run a **group‑sequential test** with O’Brien‑Fleming boundaries, checking the p‑value after every 20 000 requests per arm. The code snippet below illustrates the workflow:

```python
import pandas as pd, numpy as np, statsmodels.stats.api as sms

df = pd.read_csv('chatbot_x_ab.csv')
def interim_test(df, n):
    sub = df.iloc[:n]
    lat_a = sub[sub.arm=='A'].latency_ms
    lat_b = sub[sub.arm=='B'].latency_ms
    t, p = sms.ttest_ind(lat_a, lat_b, usevar='pooled')
    return p

for n in [20000, 40000, 60000, 80000]:
    p = interim_test(df, n*2)   # *2 because each arm contributes n rows
    print(f'Interim n={n*2}: p={p:.4f}')
```

The interim p‑values never cross the O’Brien‑Fleming efficacy boundary, so the experiment runs to completion. This disciplined monitoring prevents premature conclusions while still offering a safety net for futility.

*Paragraph 3 – Final Analysis, Bootstrapped CIs, and Decision*  
At the full sample we compute the primary statistics:

```python
from scipy import stats
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Latency
lat_a = df[df.arm=='A'].latency_ms
lat_b = df[df.arm=='B'].latency_ms
diff_lat = lat_b.mean() - lat_a.mean()
ci_lat = stats.bootstrap((lat_b - lat_a,), np.mean, confidence_level=0.95)

# Satisfaction
sat_a = df[df.arm=='A'].satisfaction_score
sat_b = df[df.arm=='B'].satisfaction_score
diff_sat = sat_b.mean() - sat_a.mean()
ci_sat = stats.bootstrap((sat_b - sat_a,), np.mean, confidence_level=0.95)

print(f'Latency Δ={diff_lat:.1f} ms, 95 % CI={ci_lat.confidence_interval}')
print(f'Satisfaction Δ={diff_sat:.3%}, 95 % CI={ci_sat.confidence_interval}')
```

Output (rounded):

```
Latency Δ=-9.2 ms, 95 % CI=(-12.8, -5.6)
Satisfaction Δ=+1.3 %, 95 % CI=(0.4 %, 2.2 %)
```

Both primary metrics satisfy the pre‑registered thresholds (≥ 5 % latency reduction ≈ 15 ms, ≥ 1 % satisfaction lift). The effect sizes (Cohen’s d ≈ 0.15 for latency, risk‑ratio ≈ 1.015 for satisfaction) are modest but economically meaningful given the scale of user traffic. The fairness flag shows a negligible increase (Δ = +0.2 %, p = 0.31), which we report as a non‑significant side effect.

The final results table, ready for the governance audit, is:

| Metric          | Δ (Treatment‑Control) | 95 % CI                | p‑value | Correction | Interpretation                              |
|-----------------|------------------------|------------------------|---------|------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Latency (ms)    | –9.2                   | (‑12.8, ‑5.6)          | 0.0012  | —          | Meaningful speed gain, meets 5 % target      |
| Satisfaction    | +1.3 %                 | (0.4 %, 2.2 %)         | 0.0420  | —          | Statistically significant, business‑impactful |
| Cost (USD)      | –0.0003                | (‑0.0005, ‑0.0001)    | 0.0035  | —          | Small cost reduction, aligns with latency   |
| Fairness flag   | +0.2 %                 | (‑0.3 %, +0.7 %)       | 0.31    | —          | No evidence of bias increase                |

Because all primary metrics pass the pre‑registered criteria and the multiple‑comparison correction is unnecessary (only one primary metric per family), the governance board authorises a full rollout of Chatbot‑X v2. ==== Philosophical Reflection

*Paragraph 1 – Evidence as the Backbone of AI Governance*  
Statistical rigor transforms anecdote into evidence, and evidence is the currency of trustworthy AI governance. When a regulator asks, “Did the new model improve user experience without harming equity?” the answer must rest on a reproducible experiment that isolates the causal impact of the change. Randomized A/B testing supplies the counterfactual, reducing the influence of hidden confounders and making the claim “Chatbot‑X v2 improves latency by 3 %” defensible to auditors, board members, and the public. Moreover, the artefacts—pre‑registration records, randomisation seeds, analysis notebooks—create a verifiable audit trail that can survive legal scrutiny.

*Paragraph 2 – The Ethics of “P‑hacking” and Selective Reporting*  
The temptation to chase a significant p‑value is especially acute in fast‑moving product teams. Imagine a fintech startup that runs ten variations of a fraud‑detection model, looks at the interim results after a day, and publishes only the variant that shows a 0.5 % reduction in false positives. This **p‑hacking** masks the true false‑positive rate and can expose customers to undetected fraud. Ethical AI governance therefore mandates **pre‑registration** of all hypotheses, **transparent reporting** of every metric (including those that did not improve), and a culture that values null findings as learning signals. In our Chatbot‑X case, we deliberately reported the non‑significant fairness flag to demonstrate that the rollout does not exacerbate bias, even though it was not a primary success metric.

*Paragraph 3 – From Statistical Significance to Responsible Decision‑Making*  
Statistical significance is a tool, not a moral verdict. A result can be statistically significant yet economically trivial (e.g., a 0.1 % latency gain that costs $1 M to implement). Conversely, a non‑significant result may hide a socially important effect when the sample is under‑powered. Responsible governance therefore interprets numbers in context: weighing effect size against deployment cost, considering equity impacts, and aligning with organisational values such as user privacy or accessibility. The Chatbot‑X rollout decision was justified not only by the p‑values but also by the calculated ROI, the negligible fairness impact, and the alignment with the company’s “fast‑but‑fair” principle. In the next lecture we will extend this reasoning to **causal inference** techniques—instrumental variables, regression discontinuity, and synthetic controls—that let us evaluate policy changes even when randomisation is infeasible.

===== Key Points

* Randomized experiments provide the evidential backbone for AI governance  
* Pre‑registration and full reporting guard against p‑hacking and selective disclosure  
* Transparency includes publishing null or adverse findings (e.g., fairness flag)  
* Distinguish statistical significance from practical/economic significance  
* Ethical responsibility to interpret effect sizes in the broader social context  
* Decision‑making integrates ROI, equity, and organisational values  
* Preview of causal‑inference methods for non‑randomized settings
