=== 1.3: The Cognitive Turn — Minds as Information Processors
:leveloffset: 2

Imagine you are solving a crossword puzzle. You read a clue, retrieve possible words from memory, test them against the intersecting letters, and update your mental notebook with each trial. Suddenly you pick a word that looks right—only to discover later that it clashes with a crossing. What invisible machinery is orchestrating this dance of perception, memory, and decision, and why does it sometimes lead us astray?

==== Conceptual Core

The story of the cognitive turn begins in the summer of 1956, when a modest group of mathematicians, psychologists, and engineers gathered at Dartmouth College under the banner “Artificial Intelligence”. Their manifesto was a daring question: *What if the brain were a digital device that could manipulate symbols the way a computer does?* The workshop produced the first public demonstration of a program called the **Logic Theorist**, a system that proved theorems from Principia Mathematica by searching through symbolic representations. The excitement in the room was palpable—participants cheered as the machine derived a proof that had taken humans weeks to discover. This event marked the birth of **cognitivism**, a research program that treats cognition as a form of information processing.

Newell and Simon quickly abstracted the success of the Logic Theorist into a bold claim: the **Physical Symbol System Hypothesis** (PSSH). In plain language, they argued that any physical system capable of creating, modifying, and combining symbolic structures has the *necessary and sufficient* means for general intelligent behavior. The hypothesis was not merely philosophical; it offered a concrete engineering target. To illustrate, consider the **General Problem Solver** (GPS), a later architecture that encoded problems as a set of initial states, goal states, and operators. GPS would systematically apply operators—symbolic “moves”—to transform the initial state toward the goal, backtracking when a dead‑end was encountered. The algorithmic elegance of GPS gave credence to the idea that a handful of well‑designed rules could, in principle, solve any problem that could be symbolically expressed.

From this symbolic foundation emerged a compact **information‑processing loop** that dominated cognitive psychology for the next two decades: **sense → represent → process → act → store**. Perception supplied raw data, which was encoded into working memory; reasoning mechanisms operated on those symbols; actions were generated; and the results were written back into long‑term memory. Cognitive architectures such as **ACT‑R** and **Soar** instantiated the loop in software, allowing researchers to simulate problem solving, decision making, and even simple language comprehension. The loop provided a shared vocabulary that linked psychology, computer science, and linguistics, and it made the mind appear as a modular machine whose parts could be measured, isolated, and recombined.

However, the map soon showed cracks. **Embodiment** scholars argued that cognition cannot be divorced from the body that houses it. The iCub robot, for example, learns to grasp a cup by repeatedly reaching, feeling resistance, and adjusting its motor commands. Each successful grasp reshapes the internal representation of “cup” in a way that no purely symbolic rule could anticipate. **Situated cognition** took the critique further, insisting that intelligence emerges from the tight coupling of agent and environment, not from internal symbols alone. A robot navigating a cluttered kitchen must constantly negotiate friction, lighting, and the expectations of human co‑habitants—variables that resist clean symbolic encoding.

The 1980s introduced a second major challenge: **connectionist** neural networks. Unlike symbolic systems that manipulate discrete symbols, connectionist models compute over distributed, sub‑symbolic activation patterns that are learned from data. The back‑propagation algorithm allowed multilayer perceptrons to acquire complex mappings without any hand‑crafted rules. This shift forced cognitivists to confront a new question: *Can intelligence arise from statistical regularities alone, or do we still need explicit symbols?* The tension persisted, and modern hybrids such as IBM’s **Watson** (which couples rule‑based reasoning with probabilistic inference) and large‑scale **transformers** (which learn soft “rules” from billions of text tokens) illustrate that the field has moved beyond the binary of symbolic versus connectionist to a spectrum of integrated approaches.

The ongoing dialogue between symbols, bodies, and statistical learning reshapes how we think about AI design. The map remains indispensable for navigation, but we must constantly redraw it as new territories—embodied robots, multimodal sensors, and self‑supervised learners—are explored.

===== Key Points

* Cognitivism emerged from a 1956 Dartmouth workshop that asked whether the brain could be treated as a digital symbol manipulator.  
* Physical Symbol System Hypothesis (Newell & Simon): symbols are *necessary and sufficient* for general intelligence.  
* General Problem Solver (GPS) demonstrated systematic symbolic search over state spaces.  
* Information‑processing loop (sense → represent → process → act → store) unified perception, memory, and reasoning.  
* ACT‑R and Soar instantiated the loop in software, enabling traceable simulations of cognition.  
* Embodiment (e.g., iCub) shows that sensorimotor interaction reshapes representations in ways symbols alone cannot capture.  
* Situated cognition stresses the inseparability of agent and environment for intelligent behavior.  
* Connectionist networks introduced distributed, learned representations that bypass explicit rule manipulation.  
* Modern hybrids (Watson, transformers) blend symbolic reasoning with statistical learning, illustrating a continuum rather than a dichotomy.  
* The map/territory metaphor reminds us that models guide exploration but never become the terrain itself.  

==== Technical Example

A concrete illustration of the symbolic tradition is a tiny rule‑based chatbot that solves the classic “Einstein’s Zebra” logic puzzle. The knowledge base contains a handful of if‑then productions such as:

```
IF house(Color) = red AND nationality(Norwegian) = house(1) THEN pet(Fish) = house(1)
IF beverage(Tea) = house(2) AND nationality(English) = house(Color) THEN color(Red) = house(2)
```

When a user submits the puzzle description, the engine parses the natural‑language clues into a set of atomic facts, places them in **working memory**, and then repeatedly scans the rule set for a match. Each time a rule fires, the corresponding fact is added to memory, and the system prints a trace line like:

```
[Rule 3] Inferred: The green house is immediately to the right of the ivory house.
```

The trace can be displayed in real time during class, allowing students to see exactly which production fired, why it matched, and how the knowledge base evolved. The transparency of this process is a pedagogical gold‑mine: learners can pinpoint the exact moment a mistaken inference occurs and discuss how to repair the rule set.

Contrast this with a modern **transformer‑based language model** that solves the same puzzle by generating a natural‑language answer. The model has never seen an explicit rule like the ones above; instead, it has internalized statistical regularities from billions of text examples. When prompted, it produces a sequence of tokens that happen to encode the correct solution. We cannot point to a single if‑then clause, but we can probe the model’s attention matrices to see which parts of the prompt influence each generated token. This “soft production system” behaves like a black box that nevertheless respects the same **input → representation → process → act → store** pipeline: the user query is embedded, the transformer processes the embedding through multiple layers, and the output token stream is returned to the user. The knowledge‑graph explorer you will use later mirrors this duality: **explicit traversal** (symbolic, fully traceable) runs alongside **learned retrieval** (connectionist, flexible but opaque).

Finally, consider a hybrid scenario where the chatbot first attempts a symbolic solve, and if it fails after a fixed number of rule firings, it falls back to the transformer to generate a candidate answer. The system then validates the candidate against the symbolic knowledge base, closing the loop with a **feedback** step that updates the rule set for future puzzles. This pattern—symbolic core plus statistical fallback—exemplifies how contemporary AI systems negotiate the trade‑off between interpretability and adaptability.

===== Key Points

* Rule‑based chatbot demo: explicit if‑then productions, working‑memory trace, live classroom visualization.  
* Production system cycle: match → fire → update memory → repeat, providing full interpretability.  
* Transformer model as a “soft” production system: learns statistical “rules” without explicit symbols, probed via attention.  
* Dual‑path pipeline in the knowledge‑graph explorer: explicit traversal (symbolic) vs. learned retrieval (connectionist).  
* Hybrid fallback strategy demonstrates practical integration of symbolic certainty with statistical flexibility.  
* Mapping to the cognitive loop: input (user query) → representation (graph or embedding) → process (traversal or neural layers) → act (answer) → store (graph updates, rule learning).  

==== Philosophical Reflection

If the mind is a computer, then a perfect simulation of every neuron’s firing pattern should, by definition, be a mind. This “full‑brain‑simulation” thought experiment forces us to ask whether computation alone can capture **consciousness**, **qualia**, and the lived sense of embodiment. John Searle’s famous **Chinese Room** argument provides a vivid counter‑example: a person inside a room follows a rulebook to manipulate Chinese symbols without ever understanding Chinese. The system passes the Turing Test for Chinese, yet most philosophers agree it lacks genuine understanding. The Chinese Room illustrates that syntactic manipulation of symbols, however sophisticated, may not generate semantic meaning—a point that resonates with the map/territory metaphor introduced earlier.

Beyond qualia, several dimensions of cognition resist pure symbol manipulation. **Embodiment** ties perception to the body’s geometry; a robot that can only manipulate symbols cannot feel the weight of a cup, nor can it adjust its grip based on tactile feedback. **Context**—the cultural, historical, and personal background that gives a crossword clue its meaning—lies outside any static knowledge graph. **Social co‑construction** means that meaning emerges through dialogue, negotiation, and shared practices, none of which are captured by a solitary symbol system. In our crossword vignette, the clue “A city that never sleeps” evokes New York only because the solver has lived in a culture where that nickname is common. A purely symbolic engine would need an external source of cultural priors to make the same inference.

Nevertheless, the map remains indispensable. Just as a cartographer’s map enables travelers to navigate unfamiliar terrain, a knowledge‑graph map enables agents—human or artificial—to traverse complex domains. The map is not the territory, but without it we would wander blindly. The challenge for AI designers, then, is to **recognise the limits of the map while exploiting its power**: to embed symbols in bodies, to enrich graphs with contextual metadata, and to couple statistical learners with transparent rule engines. In doing so we move closer to systems that not only solve puzzles but also *understand* the cultural and embodied stakes that make those puzzles meaningful.

===== Key Points

* Full‑brain‑simulation raises the question: does perfect computation equal genuine thinking?  
* Chinese Room argument shows that syntactic symbol manipulation can lack semantic understanding.  
* Missing dimensions for pure symbolic models: qualia, embodiment, contextual cultural knowledge, social co‑construction.  
* Crossword example illustrates how meaning depends on cultural and personal context beyond raw symbols.  
* Knowledge graphs act as maps: they guide navigation but never become the territory itself.  
* The map/territory metaphor urges humility: use models as guides, not as complete explanations.  
* Designing AI requires integrating symbols, bodies, and statistical learning to approach genuine understanding.
