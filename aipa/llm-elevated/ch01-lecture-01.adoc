=== 1.1: Definitions of Intelligence — Stored vs. Flowing
:leveloffset: 2

When you ask Siri for the weather, does the answer come from a stored fact sheet or from a model that “thinks” on the fly?

[.epigraph]
"The problem is not that people do not know the truth, but that they do not know that they do not know it."

==== Conceptual Core

Imagine you are playing a blitz chess game against two opponents. **Opponent A** has memorised every endgame position with the optimal move; when the board matches one of its memorised patterns, it instantly replies. **Opponent B** knows only the rules of chess, explores possible continuations on the spot, and decides the best move by evaluating the positions it generates. Both opponents win games, yet they embody two opposite ways of locating *intelligence*.

The first opponent illustrates the **stored‑representation view**: intelligence resides in *what the system has*—a knowledge base, a set of pre‑computed answers, a database of facts. The second opponent embodies the **process view**: intelligence resides in *what the system does*—the transformation of inputs into outputs through computation, feedback, and emergence.  

> **Question:** *Where does intelligence live—inside the system or in the activity of the system?*  

The classic **Turing test** asks whether a machine can imitate human conversation, but it does not tell us whether the imitation relies on stored symbols or on on‑the‑fly processing. **Searle’s Chinese Room** pushes the puzzle further: a person follows a rule book to manipulate Chinese symbols without understanding them. The room passes the Turing test, yet the *meaning* seems absent.  

If we adopt the **stored‑representation view**, the rule book itself is sufficient for “understanding.” If we adopt the **process view**, understanding emerges only during the *doing*—the manipulation, the feedback, the continual adjustment. Both perspectives capture real aspects of AI systems.  

Expert systems of the 1980s, such as **MYCIN**, leaned heavily on stored rules and frames: a physician‑level diagnosis was produced by matching a patient’s symptoms against a hand‑crafted knowledge base. By contrast, **Deep Blue** (1997) combined a massive opening book (stored) with a fast alpha‑beta search (process) to defeat Kasparov. More recently, **AlphaZero** (2017) reduced the stored component to a few hundred megabytes of network weights and let the Monte‑Carlo Tree Search *create* its own knowledge during play. Modern large language models (LLMs) blend the two: billions of parameters *store* statistical regularities, while a single forward pass *processes* that knowledge into fluent text. Recognising this blend is crucial because it shapes design choices, cost structures, and the distribution of power in AI.

A practical way to visualise the tension is to ask: **If you had to answer a question on a device with 128 MB of RAM, would you prefer a giant pre‑computed table or a lightweight algorithm that thinks each time?** The answer will differ depending on latency requirements, update frequency, and who controls the source of truth. In the next minutes we will unpack these trade‑offs, see how they appear in concrete systems, and explore what they imply for the politics of knowledge.

> **Bridge:** Understanding the stored ↔ flowing spectrum will let you decide, when you design a system, whether to invest in *more memory* or *more compute*—a decision that will echo throughout the labs and the next lecture on learning dynamics.

===== Key Points

* **Stored‑representation view:** intelligence as internal knowledge (facts, parameters, tables).  
* **Process view:** intelligence as transformation, circulation, and emergence.  
* **Turing test** probes observable behaviour, not the locus of intelligence.  
* **Chinese Room** highlights the gap between symbol manipulation and meaning.  
* Historical systems illustrate the spectrum: MYCIN (high storage), Deep Blue (mixed), AlphaZero (low storage, high process).  
* Modern LLMs are hybrid: massive parameter stores + on‑the‑fly inference.  
* Design trade‑offs (storage vs. computation) influence scalability, latency, and control.  
* Power follows the locus: centralized storage concentrates authority; distributed processing diffuses it.

==== Technical Example

Consider two approaches to a chess endgame with three pieces (king, rook, and opponent’s king). **System X** stores a *tablebase*: a pre‑computed lookup table that contains the optimal move for every legal position (≈ 4.6 million entries, ~ 300 MB on disk). When presented with a position, it simply retrieves the answer—*no computation beyond the lookup*. The latency is typically < 1 ms, but the storage cost is fixed and updating the table requires recomputation of the entire space.

**System Y** knows only the rules of chess; it performs a *search* through the state space, evaluates positions with a heuristic, and computes the best move on demand. A depth‑limited minimax with alpha‑beta pruning might explore 10⁶ nodes in 200 ms on a modern laptop. The storage footprint is tiny (the engine code and a few megabytes of opening book), but the compute cost grows dramatically with more pieces or deeper look‑ahead.

| Metric                     | System X (Tablebase) | System Y (Search) |
|----------------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Storage required           | ~ 300 MB             | ~ 5 MB            |
| Average latency per move   | < 1 ms               | 150‑250 ms        |
| Update cost (new pieces)   | Re‑compute entire table (hours) | Minor code tweak |
| Flexibility (new rules)    | Low (must rebuild)   | High (change heuristic) |

*Pseudo‑code snippet*  

```python
# System X – lookup
def best_move(position):
    return tablebase[position]          # O(1) hash lookup

# System Y – minimax search
def best_move(position, depth=5):
    def minimax(pos, d):
        if d == 0 or game_over(pos):
            return evaluate(pos)
        scores = [minimax(next_pos, d-1) for next_pos in legal_moves(pos)]
        return max(scores) if player_to_move(pos) == 'white' else min(scores)
    return argmax(legal_moves(position), lambda m: minimax(apply(position, m), depth-1))
```

The table illustrates the classic **high‑storage / low‑compute** versus **low‑storage / high‑compute** trade‑off. Neither is universally superior: tablebases excel in tiny state spaces where latency matters (e.g., endgame tablebases used by grandmasters), while search engines scale to the full game because they can prune irrelevant branches.

Hybrid designs occupy the middle ground. A modern knowledge graph, for instance, stores nodes and edges **(static structure)** but also supports **traversal, inference, and embedding** **(dynamic flow)**. A query therefore does more than retrieve—it *aggregates* information along paths, updates embeddings, and may even trigger downstream actions. In the labs you will build a tiny graph that stores entities and then runs a flow‑based query engine on top of it, mirroring the hybrid diagram you will see later.

===== Key Points

* **Lookup table (System X):** pre‑computed answers → fast retrieval, high storage.  
* **Search engine (System Y):** rules + state‑space exploration → on‑demand computation, low storage.  
* **Trade‑off:** storage vs. computation; choice depends on problem scale, latency, and update frequency.  
* **Hybrid systems** combine both: stored structure + runtime flow.  
* **Knowledge graphs** illustrate the blend of static representation and dynamic processing.  
* Quantitative comparison (size, latency, update cost) makes the abstract trade‑off concrete.  
* Pseudo‑code clarifies the algorithmic difference between lookup and search.

==== Philosophical Reflection

Knowledge is not a static commodity but a circulating medium: models produce it, users consume it, and feedback loops reshape it through retraining. No single locus holds all the knowledge; intelligence emerges from the *circuit* rather than any isolated node. Foucault’s notion of the *episteme*—the rules and practices that make certain knowledge possible—maps onto AI as an **epistemic machine** that both stores and circulates information.

A concrete case study helps to see the political stakes. In early 2023 Microsoft launched **Bing Chat**, a retrieval‑augmented chatbot that combines a large language model with a live web index. The *stored* component (the LLM) is owned and updated by OpenAI; the *flowing* component (the web retriever) is controlled by Microsoft’s search infrastructure. When a user asks “What are the latest COVID‑19 guidelines in Canada?”, the answer depends on which index the retriever consults, how often the index is refreshed, and whether the LLM’s internal parameters have been fine‑tuned on recent data. The *ownership* of the knowledge base (Microsoft) and the *ownership* of the generative model (OpenAI) are distinct, yet the user experiences a single “intelligent” system. This split creates a hidden power asymmetry: the entity that controls the *stored* knowledge can shape what is *retrievable*, while the entity that controls the *process* can shape how that knowledge is *presented*.

When intelligence is flowing, we might think it is “open” because anyone can send a query. However, the flow is mediated by APIs, rate limits, and proprietary ranking algorithms—mechanisms that re‑centralise control in the hands of the service provider. Conversely, a purely stored system (e.g., an open‑source tablebase) can be copied, audited, and run offline, diffusing power but limiting adaptability. Hybrid designs can therefore be engineered to **balance control and openness**: a community‑maintained knowledge graph (stored) paired with a lightweight local inference engine (process) lets users both audit the facts and customise the reasoning.

The stored ↔ flowing distinction therefore reveals hidden political economies of AI. It forces us to ask: *Who decides what gets stored? Who decides how the flow is shaped?* Answering these questions is essential for responsible AI design, regulation, and for the ethical stewardship of the knowledge we embed in machines.

> **Bridge:** In the upcoming lab you will experience this balance first‑hand: you will store a tiny graph of facts, then write a flow‑based query that retrieves and recombines those facts on the fly. The design choices you make will echo the power trade‑offs discussed here.

===== Key Points

* Knowledge circulates: production → consumption → reshaping (feedback).  
* Foucault’s *episteme* → AI as an epistemic machine that both stores and circulates.  
* **Stored** systems (e.g., open tablebases) concentrate control in the data owner; **flowing** systems (e.g., cloud APIs) diffuse control but embed hidden governance.  
* Retrieval‑augmented chatbots illustrate a split ownership of stored knowledge and flowing computation.  
* Hybrid designs can be engineered to balance auditability, adaptability, and power distribution.  
* Recognising the stored ↔ flowing spectrum is a prerequisite for ethical AI policy and system design.
