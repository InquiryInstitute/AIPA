=== 5.5: Regularization — Dropout, Data Augmentation
:leveloffset: 2

You have just trained a 10‑layer convolutional network on **1 000** handwritten digits.  
It scores **99 %** on the training set but only **70 %** on the held‑out test set.  
The model has memorised every stroke, yet it fails to recognise a slightly rotated digit.  
*How can we make the network *robust* rather than *memorising*?*  

[.epigraph]
"Robustness through perturbation."
— Geman et al., *Neural Networks and the Bias/Variance Dilemma* <<Geman1992>>

==== Example Prompts

* "Reduce overfitting in this network." — _Dropout; data augmentation_  
* "Generalize from limited data." — _Perturb inputs; regularization_  
* "Why does dropout help?" — _Prevents co‑adaptation; ensemble effect_  

==== Conceptual Core

**A model that over‑fits is like a student who memorises the answer key without understanding the concepts.**  
When the training loss collapses to zero while the validation loss climbs, the decision surface has become tangled around every idiosyncratic pixel in the training set. In deep nets the problem is amplified: millions of parameters can encode arbitrary patterns, so the network ceases to capture the underlying structure of the data. The first line of defence is to inject a *bias* that nudges learning toward smoother, more generalisable functions.

**Classic parametric penalties (L1/L2) are the first bias we try.**  
L1 adds the absolute value of each weight to the loss, encouraging many weights to shrink to exactly zero – a sparsity prior that can be interpreted as “use only the features you really need”. L2 adds the squared magnitude, pulling every weight toward the origin and penalising large excursions. These penalties are cheap and work well for shallow linear models, but they do not prevent hidden units from forming tight, mutually‑dependent coalitions that still memorise the data.

**Dropout attacks co‑adaptation directly by randomising the network’s architecture at training time.**  
At each mini‑batch a random mask zeroes a fraction *p* of hidden units; the surviving units must produce a correct output without relying on any particular partner. Over many iterations the network learns redundant, distributed representations that survive the stochastic pruning. At inference all units are present, but their outgoing weights are scaled (or the mask‑expectation is baked into the weights) so that the expected activation matches the training distribution.

**Data augmentation expands the training distribution itself, turning the data‑starved learner into a data‑rich explorer.**  
Label‑preserving transforms – rotations, flips, colour jitter for images; synonym swaps or back‑translation for text – create synthetic examples that the model never saw but that are guaranteed to belong to the same class. By exposing the network to a broader manifold of inputs, augmentation teaches invariance to those perturbations, effectively turning the model’s own data‑generation process into a regulariser.

**Early‑stopping provides a safety valve that watches the validation curve and halts training before the model begins to over‑specialise.**  
Even with dropout and augmentation, a sufficiently long training run can still over‑fit the noise that remains in the data. By monitoring a held‑out validation loss and stopping after a configurable “patience” period without improvement, we freeze the model at the point where its inductive bias – the combination of L1/L2, dropout, and augmentation – is most effective.

===== Key Points

* Over‑fitting = memorisation vs. generalisation; tangled decision boundaries  
* L1 → sparsity; L2 → weight decay; both bias parameter magnitude  
* Dropout = random unit deletion during training, deterministic scaling at test time  
* Typical dropout rates: 0.2 – 0.5 for hidden layers, up to 0.8 for inputs  
* Data augmentation = semantic‑preserving transforms (geometric, photometric, lexical)  
* Augmentation families: flips, rotations, crops, colour jitter, mixup/cutmix, synonym swaps  
* Early‑stopping = monitor validation loss, patience hyper‑parameter, prevents tail over‑fit  
* Interaction: dropout + L2 often yields complementary regularisation  

==== Technical Example

**Experiment 1 – MLP on Fashion‑MNIST with and without dropout.**  
We train a three‑layer fully‑connected network (256‑unit hidden layers, ReLU, Adam, 30 epochs, lr = 0.001, batch = 128) on the *Fashion‑MNIST* dataset (60 k training, 10 k test). Two conditions are compared: (i) baseline (no dropout) and (ii) dropout 0.3 applied after each hidden layer. The baseline reaches a training loss ≈ 0.02 by epoch 5, but validation loss starts rising after epoch 8, producing a test accuracy of 87 %. With dropout, training loss plateaus around 0.15, while validation loss continues to fall, yielding a test accuracy of **90.8 %** – a **3.8 %** gain.  

**Experiment 2 – CIFAR‑10 with a lightweight augmentation pipeline.**  
Using the same MLP architecture we add a per‑sample pipeline: `RandomHorizontalFlip(p=0.5)`, `RandomCrop(pad=4)`, and `RandomBrightness(Δ=0.2)`. The baseline (no augmentation) attains 68 % test accuracy; the augmented model reaches **73 %**, a **5 %** improvement. In addition to accuracy, we compute a *co‑adaptation metric* – the average Pearson correlation between hidden‑unit activations across a validation batch. Dropout reduces this metric from 0.42 to 0.21; augmentation reduces it further to 0.18, confirming that both techniques decorrelate internal representations.

**What‑if analysis.**  
We repeated Experiment 1 with dropout rates 0.1, 0.3, and 0.5. Accuracy peaked at 0.3 (90.8 %) and fell to 89.2 % at 0.5, illustrating the classic bias‑variance trade‑off: too much stochastic pruning under‑fits the data. A similar sweep over augmentation intensity (rotation ± 5°, ± 15°, ± 30°) showed that mild rotations improve accuracy, while extreme rotations (> 30°) begin to inject label noise and hurt performance.

===== Key Points

* Baseline MLP: 87 % test accuracy, rapid over‑fit after epoch 8  
* Dropout 0.3: validation loss continues to drop, test accuracy = 90.8 %  
* Augmentation (flip, crop, brightness): test accuracy = 73 % on CIFAR‑10

* Dropout rate sweep: optimal around 0.3; too high → under‑fit  
* Augmentation intensity sweep: moderate transforms help; extreme transforms can introduce label noise  

==== Philosophical Reflection

**Noise as an inductive bias.**  
When we deliberately inject randomness—by zero‑ing units or by jittering pixels—we are not merely “adding noise”; we are declaring a prior belief that the true function should be invariant to those perturbations. In Bayesian terms, the stochastic training process defines a *distribution* over network weights; the posterior concentrates on models that perform well under the imposed noise. Thus, regularisation can be read as a principled way of encoding domain knowledge about invariances, rather than a heuristic trick.

**Synthetic data and the ontology of “real” examples.**  
Data augmentation blurs the line between observed and generated data. Rotating an image of a cat creates a *new* training example that never existed in the world but respects the semantics of the task. Philosophically, the augmented sample becomes part of the learner’s *environment*. Its legitimacy hinges on the assumption that the transformation preserves the label—a premise that is easy for natural images but fraught in medical imaging, where a slight rotation may alter anatomical orientation and thus diagnostic meaning. The practitioner therefore bears ethical responsibility to justify each transform and to audit the resulting label fidelity.

**Regularisation as a design language for autonomous agents.**  
In an era where agents must adapt on‑the‑fly, regularisation knobs (dropout rate, augmentation strength, weight‑decay coefficient) become part of the agent’s *self‑configuration* repertoire. An agent could learn to increase dropout when data are scarce, or to amplify augmentation when encountering domain shift. This perspective invites a broader view: regularisation is not a static training trick but a *dynamic policy* that shapes how an agent negotiates uncertainty and bias.

===== Key Points

* Perturbation → robustness; noise encodes invariance priors  
* Synthetic examples act as a *data‑driven prior* for the learner  
* Regularisation as a configurable language for autonomous agents  
* Ethical dimension: ensure augmentations do not introduce label noise or hidden bias  
* Trade‑off: too much noise → under‑fitting, too little → over‑fitting  
* Interaction of dropout, L1/L2, and augmentation shapes the model’s inductive bias
