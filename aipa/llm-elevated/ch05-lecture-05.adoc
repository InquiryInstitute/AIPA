=== 5.5: Regularization — Dropout, Data Augmentation
:leveloffset: 2

You have just trained a 10‑layer convolutional network on **1 000** handwritten digits.  
It scores **99 %** on the training set but only **70 %** on the held‑out test set.  
The model has memorised every stroke, yet it fails to recognise a slightly rotated digit.  
*How can we make the network *robust* rather than *memorising*?*  

[.epigraph]
"Robustness through perturbation."
— Geman et al., *Neural Networks and the Bias/Variance Dilemma* <<Geman1992>>

==== Conceptual Core

**From memoriser to explorer.**  
Imagine a student who crams every lecture slide before an exam. The night of the test the professor asks a *slightly* different question, and the student falters. In the same way, a deep network that drives its training loss to zero has learned a *memoriser*—its decision surface twists around every idiosyncratic pixel. A simple visualisation on a synthetic 2‑D dataset (two interleaved spirals) shows a smooth classifier as a gentle curve, whereas an over‑fitted model produces a tangled web of narrow corridors that hug each training point. The tension between *memorisation* and *generalisation* is the central problem we must resolve.

**Why naïve weight decay is not enough.**  
The first line of defence that most textbooks teach is an L1 or L2 penalty on the parameters. L1 adds the absolute value of each weight to the loss, encouraging many weights to collapse to exactly zero; L2 adds the squared magnitude, pulling every weight toward the origin. These penalties bias the optimisation toward *smaller* parameters, which often yields smoother functions for linear models. However, in a deep net the *shape* of the decision surface is governed more by the *co‑adaptation* of hidden units than by the magnitude of any single weight. A network can still form tightly‑coupled coalitions that collectively encode each training example, even when every weight is tiny. Thus, L1/L2 alone cannot break the tangled surface we observed.

**Dropout as stochastic architecture search.**  
Dropout attacks co‑adaptation directly by randomly removing a fraction *p* of hidden units at each mini‑batch. The surviving units must produce a correct output without relying on any particular partner, forcing the network to learn *redundant, distributed* representations. Over many iterations the model behaves like an exponential ensemble of thinned subnetworks, and at inference time the expected activation is recovered by scaling the outgoing weights (or by using the built‑in expectation‑preserving behaviour of modern libraries). A quick‑poll: on a scale of 1–5, how confident are you that this stochastic pruning will improve robustness? (Discuss with your neighbour for a minute.) The answer is rarely a perfect 5; the trade‑off between *bias* (loss of capacity) and *variance* (reduced over‑fit) becomes evident as we vary *p*.

**Data augmentation: expanding the learner’s world.**  
While dropout forces the model to be robust *inside* the network, data augmentation expands the *outside*—the training distribution itself. By applying label‑preserving transforms (rotations, flips, colour jitter for images; synonym swaps or back‑translation for text) we generate synthetic examples that the model never saw but that are guaranteed to belong to the same class. In the stop‑sign example, rotating the sign by ±15° creates new training points that teach the network invariance to viewpoint changes. This process can be visualised as inflating the cloud of training points, smoothing the decision boundary in the same way a painter adds brush strokes to fill gaps. However, the assumption that a transform preserves the label is not universal; in medical imaging a 90° rotation may flip left/right anatomy, turning a benign scan into a pathological one. The ethical responsibility to audit each transform becomes part of the regularisation design.

**Early‑stopping and the forward bridge.**  
Even with dropout and augmentation, a sufficiently long training run can still chase noise that remains in the data. Early‑stopping watches a held‑out validation loss and halts training the moment improvement stalls for a configurable “patience” period. In practice this is the *safety valve* that freezes the model at the sweet spot where all three inductive biases—weight decay, stochastic pruning, and expanded data—are jointly optimal. The next lecture will show how agents can *learn* to adjust these knobs on‑the‑fly, turning regularisation from a static recipe into a dynamic policy.

===== Key Points

* Over‑fitting = memorisation vs. generalisation; tangled decision boundaries illustrated on 2‑D spirals  
* L1 → sparsity; L2 → weight decay; both bias parameter magnitude but not co‑adaptation  
* Dropout = random unit deletion each batch, forces redundant distributed representations, ensemble effect  
* Dropout trade‑off: bias‑variance curve; optimal *p* around 0.2‑0.4 for most vision tasks  
* Data augmentation = synthetic, label‑preserving transforms that expand the training manifold  
* Augmentation families: geometric (rotations, flips), photometric (brightness, colour jitter), lexical (synonym swap, back‑translation)  
* Early‑stopping = monitor validation loss, patience hyper‑parameter, prevents tail over‑fit  
* Ethical guardrail: verify that each transform truly preserves the label in the target domain  

==== Technical Example

**Experiment 1 – MLP on Fashion‑MNIST with and without dropout.**  
We train a three‑layer fully‑connected network (256‑unit hidden layers, ReLU, Adam, 30 epochs, lr = 0.001, batch = 128) on the *Fashion‑MNIST* dataset (60 k training, 10 k test). Two conditions are compared: (i) baseline (no dropout) and (ii) dropout 0.3 applied after each hidden layer. The table below reports epoch‑wise loss and accuracy for the two runs.

[cols="^.^1,^.^1,^.^1,^.^1",options="header"]
|===
| Epoch | Baseline Train Loss | Baseline Val Loss | Dropout Val Loss

| 5 | 0.02 | 0.48 | 0.62  
| 10 | 0.01 | 0.55 ↑ | 0.45 ↓  
| 15 | 0.01 | 0.61 ↑ | 0.38 ↓  
| 20 | 0.01 | 0.68 ↑ | 0.34 ↓  
| 30 | 0.01 | 0.73 ↑ | 0.31 ↓  
|===

The baseline reaches a training loss ≈ 0.02 by epoch 5, but validation loss starts rising after epoch 8, producing a test accuracy of 87 %. With dropout, training loss plateaus around 0.15, while validation loss continues to fall, yielding a test accuracy of **90.8 %** – a **3.8 %** gain. The loss curves (see Figure 5.5‑a) show the classic “U‑shaped” validation curve for the baseline and a steadily decreasing curve for the dropout model.

```python
# PyTorch snippet for dropout layer
import torch.nn as nn

class FashionMLP(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, dropout_rate=0.3):
        super().__init__()
        self.fc1 = nn.Linear(784, 256)
        self.drop1 = nn.Dropout(p=dropout_rate)
        self.fc2 = nn.Linear(256, 256)
        self.drop2 = nn.Dropout(p=dropout_rate)
        self.out = nn.Linear(256, 10)

    def forward(self, x):
        x = x.view(-1, 784)
        x = nn.ReLU()(self.fc1(x))
        x = self.drop1(x)
        x = nn.ReLU()(self.fc2(x))
        x = self.drop2(x)
        return self.out(x)
```

**Experiment 2 – CIFAR‑10 with a lightweight augmentation pipeline.**  
Using the same MLP architecture we add a per‑sample pipeline built with Albumentations:

```python
import albumentations as A
transform = A.Compose([
    A.HorizontalFlip(p=0.5),
    A.RandomCrop(width=28, height=28, p=1.0, pad_if_needed=True),
    A.RandomBrightnessContrast(brightness_limit=0.2, contrast_limit=0.2, p=0.5)
])
```

The baseline (no augmentation) attains 68 % test accuracy; the augmented model reaches **73 %**, a **5 %** improvement. In addition to accuracy, we compute a *co‑adaptation metric* – the average Pearson correlation between hidden‑unit activations across a validation batch. Dropout reduces this metric from 0.42 to 0.21; augmentation reduces it further to 0.18, confirming that both techniques decorrelate internal representations.

**What‑if analysis.**  
A sweep over dropout rates (0.1, 0.3, 0.5) shows a peak at 0.3 (90.8 %) and a decline at 0.5 (89.2 %). A sweep over rotation magnitude (±5°, ±15°, ±30°) reveals that mild rotations improve accuracy, while extreme rotations (> 30°) inject label noise and hurt performance. These results illustrate the bias‑variance trade‑off in concrete numbers and motivate the need for *adaptive* regularisation policies.

* Baseline MLP over‑fits after epoch 8; dropout keeps validation loss decreasing

==== Philosophical Reflection

**Noise as an explicit inductive bias.**  
When we deliberately inject randomness—by zero‑ing units or by jittering pixels—we are not merely “adding noise”; we are *declaring* a prior belief that the true function should be invariant to those perturbations. In Bayesian terms, the stochastic training process defines a *distribution* over network weights; the posterior concentrates on models that perform well under the imposed noise. Regularisation therefore becomes a principled way of encoding domain knowledge about invariances, rather than an ad‑hoc trick. This perspective invites us to ask: *Which invariances are truly present in the data, and which are artefacts of our modelling choices?*

**Synthetic data and the ontology of “real” examples.**  
Data augmentation blurs the line between observed and generated data. Rotating an image of a cat creates a *new* training example that never existed in the world but respects the semantics of the task. Philosophically, the augmented sample becomes part of the learner’s *environment*. Its legitimacy hinges on the assumption that the transformation preserves the label—a premise that is easy for natural images but fraught in medical imaging, where a slight rotation may alter anatomical orientation and thus diagnostic meaning. An ethical checklist for any augmentation pipeline therefore includes: (1) Does the transform preserve the semantic class? (2) Could the transform introduce hidden bias (e.g., over‑representing a demographic)? (3) Is the transformed data auditable by domain experts? (4) Does the augmentation respect regulatory constraints (e.g., patient privacy)?

**Regularisation as a dynamic design language for autonomous agents.**  
In an era where agents must adapt on‑the‑fly, regularisation knobs (dropout rate, augmentation strength, weight‑decay coefficient) become part of the agent’s *self‑configuration* repertoire. An agent could learn to increase dropout when data are scarce, or to amplify augmentation when encountering domain shift. This perspective invites a broader view: regularisation is not a static training trick but a *policy* that shapes how an agent negotiates uncertainty and bias. The upcoming lab will give you the tools to let your model *learn* its own regularisation schedule, foreshadowing research on meta‑regularisation and AutoML‑driven augmentation.

===== Key Points

* Perturbation → explicit invariance prior; noise encodes Bayesian bias  
* Synthetic examples become part of the learner’s environment; legitimacy rests on label‑preservation assumption  
* Ethical checklist for augmentation: label preservation, hidden bias, expert audit, regulatory compliance  
* Regularisation knobs can be treated as *policy parameters* that agents adapt during training or deployment  
* Trade‑off reminder: too much noise → under‑fitting, too little → over‑fitting  
* Interaction of dropout, L1/L2, and augmentation jointly shapes the model’s inductive bias
