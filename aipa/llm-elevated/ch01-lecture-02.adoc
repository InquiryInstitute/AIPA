==== Conceptual Core

*Problem 1 – The Need for Explicit Knowledge*  
In the 1960s and early 1970s most computers could only process numbers. Researchers who wanted to model a physician’s reasoning had to **write** that reasoning down. The classic example is **MYCIN**, a Lisp‑based expert system for bacterial infection diagnosis. MYCIN’s knowledge base consisted of roughly 600 if‑then rules such as:

```lisp
(if (and (fever ?p) (cough ?p)) (assert (diagnosis ?p influenza)))
```

When a doctor entered a patient’s symptoms, MYCIN traversed this rule graph, produced a confidence score, and printed a textual justification that could be inspected line‑by‑line. The system’s transparency was its selling point: a physician could see exactly which rule fired and why.

*Response 1 – The Symbolic Boom*  
The success of MYCIN inspired a wave of expert systems: **XCON** (configuration of VAX computers), **DENDRAL** (organic‑chemistry inference), and **PROSPECTOR** (geological prospecting). These systems shared three common traits:

1. **Explicit knowledge** – facts and inference rules were hand‑coded.  
2. **Logic‑driven inference** – forward or backward chaining over a symbolic graph.  
3. **Auditable trace** – every conclusion could be traced back to a source rule.

The boom peaked in the early 1980s, when governments poured funding into knowledge‑engineering labs. Yet the very strength of these systems—human‑written rules—became their Achilles’ heel. For every new domain, a team of experts and knowledge engineers had to sit down for months, translating tacit expertise into formal statements. This effort is now known as the **knowledge‑acquisition bottleneck**.

*Limitation 1 – The Bottleneck and the First AI Winter*  
By the late 1970s, the cost of maintaining and extending expert systems outstripped their benefits. MYCIN’s developers reported that adding a single new disease required rewriting dozens of rules, and the resulting system became fragile when faced with noisy, incomplete data. Funding agencies, frustrated by diminishing returns, cut back dramatically. The period 1974‑1980 is remembered as the **first AI winter**, a collective realization that **explicit** knowledge alone could not scale to the complexity of real‑world problems.

*Problem 2 – The Promise of Distributed Representations*  
Parallel to the symbolic effort, a small community explored **connectionist** ideas. Frank Rosenblatt’s **perceptron** (1958) demonstrated that a simple weighted sum could learn a linear decision boundary from examples. The 1969 *Perceptrons* book by Minsky and Papert showed the perceptron’s limits (inability to learn XOR), which dampened enthusiasm for a decade. The field waited for a learning algorithm that could adjust many weights simultaneously.

*Response 2 – The Connectionist Revival*  
The breakthrough arrived in 1986 with **Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams’** back‑propagation algorithm. By propagating error gradients through multiple layers, neural networks could learn non‑linear mappings from data. Early demos—handwritten digit recognition, speech phoneme classification—showed that a system could acquire **implicit** knowledge simply by adjusting numeric weights. The community’s excitement shifted from “coding rules” to “feeding data”.

*Limitation 2 – Opacity and the Second AI Winter*  
Neural nets, however, produced **opaque** decision boundaries. A clinician looking at a trained network could not point to a specific weight and say “this is why the patient has influenza”. When the 1990s brought the first large‑scale failures (e.g., a neural‑based credit‑scoring system that discriminated against minorities), public trust eroded. Funding again receded, ushering in the **second AI winter** (1993‑2005). Scholars began to ask whether a purely connectionist approach could ever satisfy the demand for explanation, accountability, and legal auditability.

*Problem 3 – The Rise of Data‑Rich Environments*  
The 2000s introduced massive digital corpora: electronic health records, social‑media streams, and image archives. Computing power grew exponentially, and GPUs made training deep networks feasible. **Deep learning** models—AlexNet (2012), ResNet (2015), BERT (2018)—demonstrated unprecedented performance on vision, language, and speech tasks. At the same time, **knowledge graphs** (Freebase, Wikidata, UMLS) matured, providing structured, queryable representations of world knowledge.

*Response 3 – Hybrid (Neuro‑Symbolic) AI*  
Researchers began to ask: *What if we could keep the interpretability of symbols while leveraging the pattern‑recognition power of neural nets?* The answer emerged as **neuro‑symbolic** architectures:

- **Neural Theorem Provers** (2017) used differentiable unification to prove logical statements.  
- **IBM Project Debater** (2020) combined a massive knowledge graph with a transformer‑based argument generator, citing specific graph nodes as evidence.  
- **Retrieval‑Augmented Generation** (RAG) models (2020) retrieve relevant passages from a vector‑indexed corpus before prompting a language model, ensuring that generated text is grounded in verifiable sources.

These systems treat symbols as **scaffolding**: a graph that defines entities, relations, and constraints. Neural components retrieve or embed the relevant subgraph, then generate or reason over it. The result is a pipeline that can *explain* its output (by pointing to graph nodes) while still benefiting from data‑driven generalization.

*Emergence — Hybrid AI as the New Paradigm*  
Today, most state‑of‑the‑art AI products are **hybrid**. A medical‑AI startup may store disease ontologies in a knowledge graph, use a BERT‑based encoder to embed a patient’s free‑text notes, retrieve the most relevant graph substructure, and finally generate a diagnosis with citations. The original emergency‑room tension is no longer a binary choice; it is a design space where **interpretability** and **performance** are co‑optimized.

===== Key Points

* Symbolic AI encodes **explicit** knowledge as logic, rules, and expert‑system frames.  
* Connectionist AI encodes **implicit** knowledge as distributed weights and activations.  
* Expert‑system successes (MYCIN, XCON) revealed the **knowledge‑acquisition bottleneck**, leading to the first AI winter.  
* Back‑propagation revived connectionism, but opacity caused the second AI winter.  
* The data explosion of the 2000s created a fertile ground for hybrid approaches.  
* Neuro‑symbolic systems treat symbols as *scaffolding* for learned representations, preserving traceability.  
* Modern pipelines (RAG, Neural Theorem Provers, Project Debater) illustrate concrete hybrid designs.  
* The original emergency‑room dilemma is now addressed by **jointly** optimizing interpretability and performance.  

==== Technical Example

We will build a miniature medical‑diagnosis pipeline that illustrates the three routes. The domain consists of three symptoms (fever, cough, rash) and three conditions (influenza, measles, allergy). The synthetic dataset contains 120 patient records, each a binary vector of symptoms and a one‑hot label for the condition.

| Patient | Fever | Cough | Rash | Diagnosis |
|---------|-------|-------|------|------------|
| P1      | 1     | 1     | 0    | Influenza |
| P2      | 1     | 0     | 1    | Measles |
| P3      | 0     | 0     | 1    | Allergy |
| …       | …     | …     | …    | … |

*Symbolic route* – In Lisp we encode the rule base as:

```lisp
(defrule influenza-rule
  (symptom (name fever) (value 1))
  (symptom (name cough) (value 1))
  =>
  (assert (diagnosis influenza)))

(defrule measles-rule
  (symptom (name fever) (value 1))
  (symptom (name rash) (value 1))
  =>
  (assert (diagnosis measles)))

(defrule allergy-rule
  (symptom (name rash) (value 1))
  (symptom (name cough) (value 0))
  (symptom (name fever) (value 0))
  =>
  (assert (diagnosis allergy)))
```

An inference engine walks the rule graph, fires the first matching rule, and returns a **trace** such as “Rule *influenza‑rule* fired because fever ∧ cough were present”. The trace can be printed for a physician to audit.

*Connectionist route* – A three‑layer feed‑forward network (input = 3, hidden = 5, output = 3) is trained with cross‑entropy loss. The PyTorch‑style pseudocode is:

```python
model = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Linear(3, 5), nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Linear(5, 3), nn.Softmax(dim=1)
)
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=0.01)
for epoch in range(200):
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    pred = model(symptom_tensor)
    loss = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()(pred, label_tensor)
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()
```

After training, feeding the symptom vector `[1,1,0]` yields a probability distribution `[0.78, 0.12, 0.10]`. The decision is hidden in the weight matrix; a doctor sees only the probabilities, not the reasoning steps.

*Neuro‑symbolic route* – We first construct a **knowledge graph** (KG) using RDF triples:

```
:Influenza  :hasSymptom  :Fever .
:Influenza  :hasSymptom  :Cough .
:Measles    :hasSymptom  :Fever .
:Measles    :hasSymptom  :Rash .
:Allergy    :hasSymptom  :Rash .
```

An embedding model (e.g., Sentence‑BERT) converts each symptom into a 768‑dimensional vector. A similarity search retrieves the subgraph whose symptom nodes are closest to the input vector. The LLM then receives a prompt:

```
Symptoms: fever, cough
Relevant KG nodes: Influenza (fever, cough)
Generate a diagnosis and explain using the KG.
```

The LLM outputs:

> “The combination of fever and cough matches the *influenza* pattern in the knowledge graph. Therefore, the most likely diagnosis is influenza.”

The answer is **grounded** (the KG node is cited) and **generated** (the LLM supplies fluent natural language). This hybrid pipeline preserves the audit trail of the symbolic route while leveraging the pattern‑recognition power of the neural route.

===== Key Points

* Symbolic expert system: transparent rule firing, but brittle to novel symptom combinations.  
* Neural classifier: learns from data, yields probability scores, but provides no human‑readable justification.  
* Neuro‑symbolic pipeline: knowledge graph (symbolic) + embedding retrieval (connectionist) + LLM generation (connectionist) → explanation grounded in graph nodes.  
* Retrieval‑augmented generation ensures that every answer can be traced back to a specific KG triple.  
* The lab will implement the KG component, then connect it to a pre‑trained embedding model for retrieval.  
* This example mirrors real‑world systems such as IBM Project Debater and RAG‑based chat assistants.  

==== Philosophical Reflection

Lyotard warned against “incredulity toward meta‑narratives”: the seductive story that AI will march linearly from hand‑crafted symbols to autonomous neurons to a singular super‑intelligence. The historical record, however, tells a **pluralistic** tale. Symbolic systems never vanished; they persisted in databases, ontologies, and the very programming languages that undergird neural frameworks. Connectionist models added a **new representational layer**—distributed weights—without erasing the older symbolic scaffolding.

Derrida’s claim that “there is nothing outside the text” invites us to treat every AI artifact—whether a rule or a weight—as a **trace** mediating between the world and our models. In the emergency‑room scenario, the “text” is the patient’s clinical narrative; the AI’s output is a trace that must be both **readable** (the physician can follow it) and **effective** (it must lead to correct treatment). Choosing a purely symbolic trace would guarantee readability but might miss subtle patterns hidden in large datasets. Choosing a purely connectionist trace would capture those patterns but leave the physician with an opaque probability cloud.

Hybrid AI embraces **multiple traces** simultaneously. By grounding neural retrieval in a knowledge graph, we create a **dual‑layered epistemic surface**: the graph offers a *canonical* representation of medical knowledge, while the embeddings capture *statistical regularities* observed in practice. This duality mirrors Foucault’s power‑knowledge: the graph encodes institutional authority (clinical guidelines), whereas the neural component reflects the *disciplinary* power of data‑driven practice. The ethical stakes become clear: a system that can point to the exact guideline it invoked (symbolic trace) is more amenable to accountability, while a system that can adapt to emerging disease patterns (connectionist trace) is more resilient.

The myth of a single “true” AI approach obscures the pragmatic reality that **design choices are always about which trace we foreground for a given stakeholder**. A radiologist may demand a heat‑map overlay (connectionist trace) alongside a textual report citing the relevant anatomical ontology (symbolic trace). A regulator, by contrast, may only accept the ontology citation. By acknowledging the trace‑like nature of both symbols and weights, we can deliberately engineer systems that satisfy **explainability**, **adaptability**, and **trust**—the three pillars of responsible AI in high‑stakes domains.

Looking ahead, the next lecture will explore **training the retrieval component** that bridges the symbolic KG and the neural encoder. In Lab 1 you will see how the very act of building the KG determines which traces are available for later explanation, turning today’s philosophical discussion into tomorrow’s concrete implementation.

===== Key Points

* Lyotard’s “incredulity” reminds us that no single paradigm can dominate the AI narrative.  
* Derrida’s “trace” frames both rules and weights as mediations, not direct access to reality.  
* Hybrid systems maintain **dual traces**: symbolic (canonical knowledge) + connectionist (statistical regularities).  
* Ethical accountability hinges on the ability to surface the symbolic trace for auditors.  
* Adaptability to new data relies on the connectionist trace’s capacity to learn from examples.  
* Designing AI is a negotiation of stakeholder priorities: clinicians, regulators, patients.  
* The upcoming lab will make the symbolic trace concrete, enabling the later neural trace to attach to it.
