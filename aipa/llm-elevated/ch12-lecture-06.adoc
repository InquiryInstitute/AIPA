==== Conceptual Core

*Hook – a story that forces the question of “why does the thesis matter?”*  
You have just finished a working prototype that reduces hospital readmission rates by 15 %. Your advisor smiles, runs a quick demo, and then asks, “Can you convince anyone else that this works?” The next day the department chair emails you: *“Your prototype is impressive, but the committee will reject the thesis unless the evaluation is reproducible and the design decisions are fully justified.”* In that moment the prototype is no longer a private triumph; it becomes a public claim that must survive scrutiny. The tension between a brilliant piece of engineering and a defensible scholarly record is the engine of every graduate thesis.

*Why a thesis is more than a grade*  
A thesis is a **portable artifact of competence**. It signals to future employers, collaborators, and reviewers that you can (1) identify a real problem, (2) devise a solution, (3) implement it, (4) evaluate it rigorously, and (5) communicate the whole process with enough detail that others can repeat it. In the academic ecosystem the thesis also serves as a **citable contribution** that can be built upon, a **record of accountability** that protects the community from unreproducible hype, and a **bridge to future work** that outlines open questions and next steps. Because the thesis lives beyond the semester, its quality directly influences career trajectories, grant eligibility, and the credibility of the field.

*The thesis as an iterative design cycle*  
The familiar linear list—Problem → Approach → Implementation → Evaluation → Discussion—can be misleading if presented as a one‑pass pipeline. In practice each section is drafted, reviewed, and revised many times, often in a different order than the list suggests. A clear problem statement may reveal hidden assumptions that force a redesign of the approach; early evaluation results may uncover implementation bugs that require a new architecture diagram; reviewer comments on the discussion may push you back to the problem to sharpen the motivation. Thinking of the thesis as a **design cycle** makes the writing process an integral part of the research, not a post‑mortem.

*Reproducibility, citations, and visual communication*  
A reproducible thesis supplies (a) raw data or a link to a data repository, (b) the exact software environment (versions, dependencies, container images), and (c) scripts that transform data into the reported figures. Citations must be precise, using a consistent style and linking every claim to prior work. Figures—architecture diagrams, performance plots, user study heatmaps—are not decorative; they are **semantic anchors** that allow readers to grasp complex ideas quickly. An appendix can host code snippets, full prompts, or additional experimental runs without cluttering the main narrative.

*Common pitfalls and strategies for success*  
Graduate students often fall into two extremes: **over‑detailing** (including every log file, which drowns the reader) or **under‑detailing** (leaving out critical hyper‑parameters, which destroys reproducibility). The sweet spot is achieved by (1) drafting a **concise problem paragraph** (150–200 words) that answers *what, for whom, and why*; (2) using a **checklist** for each section (see the Technical Example); (3) seeking **early feedback** from advisors, peers, and even non‑technical friends; and (4) iterating on **visuals**—a good figure often reveals missing narrative steps. Remember: *writing is thinking*; each revision forces you to resolve ambiguities that would otherwise remain hidden in the code.

*Closing bridge to the lab*  
By the end of today’s session you will have a **one‑page outline** that maps your own capstone onto the design‑cycle framework, a **reproducibility checklist** tailored to your domain, and a **draft problem paragraph** ready for peer review. In Lab 3 you will transform this outline into a 2‑page draft, exchange it with a partner, and practice responding to reviewer comments—exactly the loop that turns a prototype into a scholarly contribution.

* Hook: scenario that forces the question “why does the thesis matter?”
* Thesis as a portable artifact of competence, accountability, and bridge to future work
* Thesis writing is an iterative design cycle, not a linear checklist
* Reproducibility checklist: data, environment, scripts

==== Technical Example

*Dissecting a real abstract*  
> *“We present **MediReduce**, a reinforcement‑learning‑based decision support system that predicts optimal discharge timing for heart‑failure patients. Using a retrospective cohort of 12 000 admissions from three hospitals, MediReduce achieved a 15 % reduction in 30‑day readmission rates compared to standard practice (p < 0.01). The system integrates electronic health record data, a calibrated Markov decision process, and a clinician‑in‑the‑loop interface. A user study with 20 physicians demonstrated a 2‑minute average decision‑making speedup without compromising safety.”*  

The abstract follows the design‑cycle language: (1) **Problem** (readmission rates), (2) **Approach** (reinforcement learning, Markov decision process), (3) **Implementation** (integration with EHR, interface), (4) **Evaluation** (statistical reduction, user study), (5) **Discussion** (speedup, safety). Notice the precise **quantitative claims**, the **cohort size**, and the **p‑value**—all elements that make the claim reproducible and compelling. When you write your own abstract, ask: *What is the concrete metric? What dataset supports it? What human factor study validates it?*  

*Typical page‑budget table*  

[cols="1,1,1", options="header"]
|===
|Section |Typical pages (30‑page thesis) |Purpose

|Problem |3–5 |Define scope, stakeholders, significance
|Approach |4–6 |Design rationale, methodology, related work
|Implementation |6–8 |Architecture, components, code excerpts
|Evaluation |5–7 |Metrics, experiments, validation, user feedback
|Discussion |3–4 |Limitations, future work, broader impact
|===

Use this table as a **planning scaffold**. For a 30‑page limit, you can allocate roughly 20 % of the total length to the problem and discussion, and the remaining 80 % to the technical core.

Answer three questions: (1) *What is the concrete problem?* (2) *Who experiences it and why does it matter?* (3) *What gap in the literature does it fill?* Pair up, exchange drafts, and use the rubric below to give each other one concrete improvement (e.g., add a stakeholder statistic, clarify the “why”, tighten the scope).

* Real abstract shows design‑cycle language and quantitative rigor
* Page‑budget table guides allocation of space per section
* Mini‑exercise: write and peer‑review a problem paragraph
* Checklist for each section (problem, approach, implementation, evaluation, discussion)

==== Philosophical Reflection

*Writing as cognition*  
The phrase “writing is thinking” is supported by cognitive research on the **generation effect**: learners who generate material (by writing) recall it better than those who merely read it. When you translate a vague mental model into sentences, you must choose terminology, order arguments, and resolve contradictions. This externalization makes hidden assumptions visible, forcing you to either justify or discard them. In the context of a thesis, the act of drafting each section is a **thinking‑through** of the research decisions you already made, often revealing design flaws before they become costly implementation bugs.

*Ethical responsibility of public claims*  
A thesis is a **public claim** that your solution works under the conditions you describe. In an era of reproducibility crises across AI, psychology, and biomedical research, the ethical stakes are high. By providing full data, code, and environment specifications, you enable others to verify, extend, or refute your work. This transparency protects the community from “black‑box” hype and builds trust with stakeholders outside academia—industry partners, policymakers, and the public. Moreover, acknowledging limitations in the Discussion is not a sign of weakness; it is an act of intellectual honesty that guides future researchers toward the most promising avenues.

*Reflective prompt*  
Imagine that ten years from now a historian of AI discovers only a single paragraph of your thesis, preserved in a digital archive. *What sentence would you want that paragraph to contain?* Would it convey the core problem, the novelty of your approach, or the societal impact you hoped to achieve? This thought experiment highlights the **lasting narrative power** of the thesis: beyond the technical details, it is a story you tell about why you mattered at a particular moment in the evolution of the field.

===== Key Points

* Writing activates the generation effect, improving recall and exposing hidden assumptions
* Thesis as a public claim carries ethical responsibility for reproducibility and transparency
* Acknowledging limitations is intellectual honesty, not weakness
* Reflective prompt emphasizes lasting narrative and societal impact

==== Lab Prep

In Lab 3 you will turn today’s outline into a **two‑page draft** that includes a problem paragraph, a concise approach description, and a sketch of your evaluation plan. Follow these steps:

1. **Outline** – Use the page‑budget table to allocate space for each section of your capstone.  
2. **Draft** – Write a 150‑word problem paragraph (you already drafted this in class) and a 250‑word approach paragraph.  
3. **Peer Review** – Pair up, exchange drafts, and apply the rubric: (a) clarity of problem, (b) justification of approach, (c) evidence of reproducibility.  
4. **Iterate** – Incorporate feedback, add a minimal figure (e.g., system architecture) and a reproducibility checklist.  
5. **Submit** – Upload the two‑page draft to the course portal; the instructor will provide a reviewer comment sheet for the next class.

* Draft problem and approach paragraphs in class
* Peer‑review using a focused rubric
* Add a minimal figure and reproducibility checklist
* Submit draft for instructor feedback; prepare to respond to reviewer comments
