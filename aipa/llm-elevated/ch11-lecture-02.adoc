==== Hook: A Runaway Budget in Real Time

Imagine a fledgling biotech startup that has just built an autonomous literature‑review agent. The agent is given a single prompt: *“Summarize the latest CRISPR papers and suggest three novel experiments.”* The code looks innocent—loop over a list of keywords, call the LLM for each, store the result, and repeat until the “novelty score” stops improving. Within ten minutes the agent has made **3 200** API calls, consumed **2.8 million** tokens, and silently charged the company **$12 800**—far beyond the $5 000 seed‑fund budget. No human ever saw the cost meter; the agent kept iterating because its internal “improvement loop” never hit a hard stop.

*Who should have been warned?* *How could the same system have been kept within budget while still delivering value?* These questions frame today’s lecture. We will explore the economic levers—cost tracking, budgeting, alerts, and rate limiting—that turn a runaway spend into a predictable, sustainable service.

==== Conceptual Core

*Problem Space: Invisible Spend in Autonomous Agents*  
Every LLM call consumes a measurable resource: tokens, compute cycles, and ultimately dollars. In a single‑user workflow the cost is easy to eyeball, but in multi‑agent pipelines the expenditure becomes a hidden variable that can explode without a clear “stop” condition. Autonomous agents amplify this risk because they can generate their own sub‑tasks, retry failures, or enter feedback loops that a human operator never observes. The first step in governance is to **make spend visible** at the granularity needed for decision‑making—per user, per task, per model, and per time window.

*Control Lever 1 – Fine‑Grained Cost Tracking*  
Cost tracking is the systematic logging of every resource‑consuming event. For LLMs this means recording:  
1. Model name (e.g., `gpt‑4‑turbo`).  
2. Tokens in and tokens out.  
3. Model‑specific price per token (often tiered: prompt vs. completion).  
4. Timestamp, user identifier, and task identifier.  
5. Ancillary compute cost (GPU‑seconds, inference latency) when self‑hosted models are used.  
By persisting these fields in a structured log (JSON, relational table, or time‑series store) we can compute **aggregate metrics** such as daily spend per user, monthly spend per department, or per‑task cost breakdowns. The visibility enables downstream policies like budgeting and alerts.

*Control Lever 2 – Budgeting, Alerts, and Soft/Hard Stops*  
A budget is a pre‑agreed ceiling expressed in dollars (or token equivalents) for a given entity (user, API key, project). Budgets can be **static** (e.g., $100 / day) or **dynamic** (adjusted based on historical usage or priority). Once a budget is defined, the system must enforce **threshold alerts** (e.g., 80 % of budget) and **action points** (soft warning at 100 %, hard stop at 110 %). Alerts can be delivered via email, Slack, or a dashboard widget, giving stakeholders the chance to intervene before a catastrophic spend. Hard stops are implemented by rejecting further requests with a `402 Payment Required` response, while soft warnings may return a `200 OK` with a warning payload.

*Control Lever 3 – Rate Limiting as a Guardrail*  
Rate limiting caps the **frequency** of requests, typically expressed as requests per minute (RPM) or per second (RPS). It serves three complementary purposes:  
1. **Prevent runaway loops** where an agent repeatedly retries a failed call.  
2. **Mitigate abuse** from malicious actors attempting denial‑of‑service attacks.  
3. **Smooth demand** to avoid sudden spikes that could overwhelm downstream compute resources.  
Rate limits can be applied at different scopes: per‑user, per‑API‑key, per‑task, or even per‑model (cheaper models may have higher limits). Modern implementations use token‑bucket or leaky‑bucket algorithms stored in fast caches (Redis, Memcached) to enforce limits with millisecond latency.

*Design Trade‑offs: Cost vs. Capability vs. Equity*  
Every lever introduces a trade‑off. Tight budgets and low RPMs reduce spend but may starve high‑value tasks of the compute they need. Conversely, generous limits increase capability but risk budget overruns and inequitable access—wealthier teams can consume disproportionate resources. Designers must balance **performance**, **cost efficiency**, and **fairness**. Techniques such as **cost‑aware prompt engineering** (using cheaper models for classification, caching repeated queries, batching multiple prompts) help shift the Pareto frontier, delivering more capability per dollar while preserving equitable access.

===== Key Points

* Invisible spend in autonomous pipelines can explode without explicit monitoring.  
* Fine‑grained cost tracking logs model, token counts, price tiers, timestamps, and compute usage.  
* Budgets are expressed in dollars or token equivalents and enforce soft warnings and hard stops.  
* Rate limiting caps request frequency, preventing loops, abuse, and resource spikes.  
* Trade‑offs must be evaluated among cost, capability, and equitable access.  
* Cost‑aware design patterns (caching, batching, model selection) shift the cost‑capability curve.  
* Dynamic policies (e.g., adjusting limits based on load) enable responsive governance.  
* Alerts should be multi‑channel (email, Slack, dashboard) to give timely human oversight.  

==== Technical Example

*Logging and Cost Ledger*  
Below is a concrete Python decorator that records every LLM invocation to a JSON‑line cost ledger. The decorator extracts the model name, token counts (using the `tiktoken` library), looks up the per‑token price from a configuration map, computes the dollar cost, and writes a structured entry to `cost_ledger.jsonl`.

```python
import json, time, functools
import tiktoken

# Model‑price map (USD per 1 000 tokens)
PRICE_TABLE = {
    "gpt-4-turbo": {"prompt": 0.03, "completion": 0.06},
    "gpt-3.5-turbo": {"prompt": 0.0015, "completion": 0.002},
    "llama-2-7b": {"prompt": 0.0004, "completion": 0.0006},
}

def cost_tracker(func):
    @functools.wraps(func)
    def wrapper(*args, **kwargs):
        model = kwargs.get("model", "gpt-3.5-turbo")
        prompt = kwargs.get("prompt", "")
        start = time.time()
        response = func(*args, **kwargs)               # actual LLM call
        elapsed = time.time() - start

        # Token counting
        enc = tiktoken.encoding_for_model(model)
        prompt_tokens = len(enc.encode(prompt))
        completion_tokens = len(enc.encode(response["content"]))

        # Pricing
        price_cfg = PRICE_TABLE.get(model, PRICE_TABLE["gpt-3.5-turbo"])
        cost = (prompt_tokens / 1_000) * price_cfg["prompt"] + \
               (completion_tokens / 1_000) * price_cfg["completion"]

        # Ledger entry
        entry = {
            "timestamp": time.time(),
            "user_id": kwargs.get("user_id", "anonymous"),
            "task_id": kwargs.get("task_id", "unknown"),
            "model": model,
            "prompt_tokens": prompt_tokens,
            "completion_tokens": completion_tokens,
            "cost_usd": round(cost, 6),
            "latency_s": round(elapsed, 3)
        }
        with open("cost_ledger.jsonl", "a") as f:
            f.write(json.dumps(entry) + "\n")
        return response
    return wrapper
```

*Rate Limiter Middleware*  
A Redis‑backed token‑bucket limiter enforces per‑user RPM limits. The snippet shows how to acquire a permit before calling the LLM; if the bucket is empty, the request is rejected with HTTP 429.

```python
import redis, time
r = redis.StrictRedis(host="localhost", port=6379, db=0)

def rate_limiter(user_id, rpm=30):
    bucket_key = f"rl:{user_id}"
    now = int(time.time())
    # Refill tokens: one token per (60/rpm) seconds
    refill_interval = 60 / rpm
    last_refill = r.hget(bucket_key, "ts")
    tokens = int(r.hget(bucket_key, "tokens") or 0)

    if last_refill:
        elapsed = now - int(last_refill)
        new_tokens = min(rpm, tokens + int(elapsed / refill_interval))
        tokens = new_tokens
    else:
        tokens = rpm

    if tokens <= 0:
        return False  # rate limit exceeded

    # Consume a token
    r.hmset(bucket_key, {"tokens": tokens - 1, "ts": now})
    r.expire(bucket_key, 120)  # keep bucket for 2 minutes
    return True
```

*Putting It Together: Budget Enforcement*  
The following function demonstrates a **soft‑hard stop** policy. It reads the cumulative spend for the user from the ledger (cached in Redis for speed), compares it against the user’s daily budget, and decides whether to allow the request, issue a warning, or reject it outright.

```python
DAILY_BUDGET = 50.0  # USD

def enforce_budget(user_id, cost):
    budget_key = f"budget:{user_id}"
    spent = float(r.get(budget_key) or 0.0) + cost
    r.set(budget_key, spent, ex=86400)  # reset each day

    if spent > DAILY_BUDGET * 1.10:
        raise Exception("402 Payment Required – budget exceeded")
    elif spent > DAILY_BUDGET * 0.80:
        # soft warning – could be logged or sent to a webhook
        print(f"⚠️ Warning: 80% of daily budget used ({spent:.2f}$)")
    return True
```

*What‑If Scenario: Sudden Price Surge*  
Suppose the provider announces a 50 % price increase for `gpt‑4‑turbo` tokens. Because the cost tracker reads prices from the `PRICE_TABLE` at runtime, the **next call** automatically reflects the new cost, and the budget enforcement logic will trigger warnings earlier. This demonstrates the importance of **dynamic price lookup** rather than hard‑coding costs.

===== Key Points

* Decorator records model, token counts, price tier, latency, and user/task identifiers.  
* JSON‑line ledger enables fast aggregation (daily spend, per‑model cost).  
* Redis token‑bucket implements per‑user RPM limits with millisecond latency.  
* Budget enforcement reads cumulative spend, issues soft warnings at 80 % and hard stops at 110 %.  
* Dynamic price tables allow the system to adapt instantly to provider price changes.  
* Combining cost tracking, rate limiting, and budget checks creates a three‑layer guardrail.  
* Alerts can be routed to Slack, email, or a monitoring dashboard for human oversight.  

==== Philosophical Reflection

*Economic Governance as Access Control*  
When a university research group receives a grant that covers only $2 000 of LLM usage per month, the **cost‑governance mechanisms** become a de‑facto gatekeeper. If the budget is exhausted, the group’s experiments halt, while a commercial partner with an unlimited contract continues unabated. In this sense, **cost is not merely a technical metric; it is a political instrument** that determines who participates in the AI knowledge economy. The EU AI Act (Art. 9 – “Economic Impact”) and the NIST AI RMF (Subcategory RM‑3 “Resource Management”) explicitly recognize that equitable access requires transparent, auditable cost controls.

*Equity vs. Efficiency Tension*  
A strict budget protects the provider’s financial sustainability but can disproportionately penalize under‑funded innovators. Conversely, a lax budget maximizes utilization but risks “resource hoarding” by well‑capitalized actors, leading to a **digital divide** in AI research. Philosophically, we must ask: *Should cost‑based throttling be supplemented with fairness‑aware policies that allocate a minimum quota to all users, regardless of ability to pay?* Some cloud providers already offer “free‑tier” quotas that guarantee a baseline of compute; however, these are often insufficient for serious research. Embedding **fairness‑aware throttling**—where a portion of the budget is reserved for low‑income users—could reconcile efficiency with social justice.

*Governance Simulator as a Laboratory for Moral Choices*  
The governance simulator introduced in Lab 1 is more than a coding exercise; it is a **sandbox for ethical experimentation**. By toggling budget caps, rate‑limit thresholds, and alert policies, students can observe how different configurations affect *who* gets service and *how* system performance degrades under stress. For instance, raising the per‑user RPM from 10 to 100 may double throughput but also cause a 30 % increase in total spend, forcing a trade‑off analysis. Such hands‑on exploration mirrors real‑world policy deliberations where regulators, providers, and users negotiate the acceptable balance between **innovation** and **resource stewardship**.

===== Key Points

* Cost governance directly shapes who can access AI services, acting as a form of access control.  
* EU AI Act Art. 9 and NIST AI RMF RM‑3 embed economic considerations within broader governance frameworks.  
* Strict budgets protect sustainability but risk exacerbating the digital divide.  
* Fairness‑aware throttling can reserve a baseline quota for under‑funded users.  
* The governance simulator lets students experiment with moral trade‑offs in a safe environment.  
* Policy questions: Should public funding mandate minimum AI compute quotas?  
* Economic governance is inseparable from ethical governance in AI ecosystems.
