=== 1.1: Definitions of Intelligence — Stored vs. Flowing

[.epigraph]
____
“Intelligence is not a thing you possess, but a flow you sustain.” — J. M. Klein (adapted)

==== Hook
* What if your chess opponent never learns, but always calculates the perfect move in real time?
* Siri can answer a question instantly, yet it never “remembers” the conversation after you hang up.
* Imagine a robot that must choose between loading a massive knowledge base or streaming sensory data on the fly.

==== Conceptual Core

When we ask *what* intelligence is, we often imagine a library of facts neatly shelved on a shelf. That picture works well for early symbolic systems, where a rule base or a lookup table is the *stored* core of the agent. Yet the same question can be turned on its head: what if intelligence is less a static archive and more a river that never stops moving? In contemporary AI, both images coexist, and the tension between them shapes every design decision we make.  

The first way to make sense of this tension is to distinguish **stored** from **flowing** resources. A *stored* resource is any representation that remains largely unchanged while the system is idle—weight matrices, symbol tables, pre‑computed endgame databases. They are the “knowledge warehouses” that an agent can query at negligible computational cost. By contrast, a *flowing* resource is a process that continuously transforms raw inputs into actions: a Monte‑Carlo tree search that builds a new search tree for each move, or a recurrent network that updates its hidden state at every timestep. The flow is the *metabolism* of information, consuming energy (compute) to keep the system alive.

The **lookup–computation spectrum** makes this dichotomy concrete. At one extreme sit static tablebases, such as the 7‑piece chess endgame database, where every possible position has a pre‑computed optimal move. No calculation is required at run time; the system merely retrieves a value. At the opposite extreme lies pure search, where the agent constructs a decision tree from scratch for each problem instance, expending considerable FLOPs but never relying on stored answers. Most modern systems sit somewhere in the middle, blending both poles.

Historically, **symbolic AI** championed the stored view. Knowledge was encoded as discrete symbols, rules, and ontologies—think of expert systems that consulted a knowledge base to diagnose a disease. **Connectionist AI**, on the other hand, emphasized distributed processing: neural weights encode patterns that are *read* and *modified* on the fly, and learning is a continuous flow of gradient updates. The rise of **hybrid systems**—for example, AlphaZero, which couples a pre‑trained neural network (stored) with Monte‑Carlo tree search (flowing)—shows that the field has moved beyond the binary and embraced a spectrum.

The **metabolism metaphor** deepens our understanding. Just as a living organism must balance energy intake, storage, and expenditure, an AI system must allocate memory (the “fat reserves”) and compute (the “muscle work”) to meet performance goals. A system with abundant storage can answer queries instantly but may become brittle when the world changes. A system that leans on flow can adapt quickly but may suffer from latency or instability. These trade‑offs echo classic engineering dilemmas: storage cost vs. latency, robustness vs. flexibility, explainability vs. adaptability.

Finally, we should view intelligence as **infrastructure**. In modern enterprises, AI components are not isolated curiosities; they are pipelines that move data, decisions, and authority across organizational boundaries. A static ontology may encode regulatory rules, while a streaming inference engine propagates those rules to downstream services in real time. Recognizing this infrastructural role helps us ask the right questions about governance, auditing, and societal impact.

==== Technical Example

To see the stored–flowing continuum in action, let us start with the **chess tablebase**. A 7‑piece endgame tablebase enumerates every legal configuration of those pieces and stores the optimal move for each. The data structure is essentially a gigantic dictionary: given a board encoding, the engine returns a move in constant time. No search, no evaluation function—just a pure *lookup*. The cost is paid upfront: generating the table required exhaustive retrograde analysis and terabytes of storage, but once built, the system can answer millions of queries per second with negligible latency.

Contrast this with a classic **AlphaBeta search** augmented by a heuristic evaluation. Here the engine receives a board position and recursively explores the game tree, pruning branches that cannot affect the final decision. The computation is *dynamic*: each move triggers a new traversal, and the amount of work depends on the depth limit, branching factor, and quality of the heuristic. There is no pre‑computed answer; the intelligence emerges from the *flow* of computation through the tree. Performance is measured in FLOPs and latency, not in megabytes of memory.

A modern hybrid, **AlphaZero**, illustrates how stored and flowing components can be woven together. The system first trains a deep neural network on self‑play games; the resulting weights constitute a *stored* knowledge base that encodes positional intuition. When faced with a new position, AlphaZero runs a **Monte‑Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)** that uses the network’s policy and value predictions to guide simulations. Each simulation is a *flowing* operation, expanding the tree and back‑propagating results. The final move is chosen by aggregating the statistics of many simulations, effectively blending stored intuition with on‑the‑fly reasoning.

A non‑gaming example is **Siri’s intent classification**. The language model that maps utterances to intents is a static set of parameters—*stored* knowledge. However, once the intent is identified, Siri may issue real‑time API calls to weather services, calendar systems, or third‑party apps. Those calls constitute a *flow* of information that depends on the current context, network latency, and external data freshness. The overall user experience hinges on balancing the quickness of the stored model with the responsiveness of the flowing services.

These examples also highlight divergent **performance metrics**. For stored systems we care about memory footprint, cache efficiency, and query throughput. For flowing systems we monitor FLOPs, wall‑clock latency, and scalability under load. Understanding which metric dominates in a given scenario guides architectural choices.

===== Key Points
* Chess tablebase: exhaustive lookup of all positions up to 7 pieces—pure storage.
* AlphaBeta search with heuristic evaluation: computation over a branching tree—pure flow.
* AlphaZero’s neural network + MCTS: pre‑trained weights (stored) plus Monte‑Carlo simulations (flowing).
* Siri’s intent classification: static language model (stored) plus real‑time API calls (flowing).
* Performance metrics differ: memory footprint for stored, FLOPs and latency for flowing.
* Example code snippet contrast: dictionary lookup vs. recursive search function.

==== Philosophical Reflection

If we accept the **metabolism metaphor**, the first philosophical question is: what does “health” mean for an AI system? In biology, health is a balance between anabolic (building) and catabolic (breaking down) processes, with waste products cleared efficiently. For an AI, the “anabolic” side might be the accumulation of reliable knowledge—well‑curated ontologies, robust weight checkpoints—while the “catabolic” side is the continual pruning of outdated models, the forgetting of noisy data, and the disposal of stale cache entries. A healthy system therefore maintains a steady flow of updates without overwhelming its storage capacity, and it discards entropy‑inducing artifacts before they corrupt decision‑making.

The **ethical implications** of stored versus flowing intelligence become stark when we consider reliability. A purely stored system, such as a legal rule engine, offers predictability and auditability: every decision can be traced back to a specific clause. However, it risks **ossification**—the inability to adapt when laws change or when novel situations arise. Conversely, a flowing system, like a real‑time recommendation algorithm, can quickly incorporate new trends but may exhibit **volatility**, producing erratic outputs that are hard to explain. Stakeholders must weigh the desire for adaptability against the need for accountability, especially in high‑stakes domains like healthcare or criminal justice.

Viewing AI as an **activity rather than a property** also reshapes our legal and economic discourse. If intelligence is a *process* that circulates information, then ownership may shift from static datasets to the *pipelines* that move data. Companies that control the flow—cloud providers, API aggregators—hold a different kind of power than those that merely store large corpora. This reconceptualization invites new regulatory frameworks that focus on the *governance of flows* (e.g., data provenance, latency guarantees) rather than solely on data licensing.

Moreover, AI systems act as **epistemic machines** that reconfigure institutional authority. When a university adopts an automated plagiarism detector, the detector does not simply store a list of known texts; it continuously evaluates new submissions, redistributing evaluative power from human reviewers to an algorithmic flow. The institution’s knowledge base becomes a living conduit, reshaping how expertise is recognized and how decisions are legitimized.

The metaphor, however, is not without limits. Biological metabolism inevitably produces waste—heat, carbon dioxide, cellular debris—that must be expelled. In AI, “entropy” appears as model drift, data bias accumulation, or computational overhead that degrades performance. Recognizing these analogues prompts us to design *garbage‑collection* mechanisms: periodic retraining, pruning of network weights, and de‑duplication of knowledge graphs. Ignoring entropy leads to systems that degrade silently, much like a body succumbing to metabolic disorders.
