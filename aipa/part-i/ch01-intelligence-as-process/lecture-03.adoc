==== Conceptual Core

The **cognitive turn** in artificial intelligence marks a decisive shift from viewing the mind as a static library of facts to treating it as a dynamic **information processor**. Early AI research, inspired by the logic of formal systems, built machines that manipulated symbols according to pre‑written rules. In that paradigm, intelligence was essentially a matter of how much could be *stored* and subsequently *queried*. The moment we began to ask how mental activity *flows*—how activation spreads, how patterns self‑organise, how learning reshapes internal structures—the picture changed dramatically.

One way to make this transition concrete is to contrast two archetypal systems. A classic symbolic parser holds a grammar in a lookup table; when a sentence arrives, the parser walks the table, matching tokens to productions. The knowledge resides in the table, and the computation is a deterministic traversal. By contrast, a modern recurrent neural network (RNN) contains a weight matrix that encodes statistical regularities gleaned from massive corpora. When a sequence of characters is fed in, the network’s hidden state *flows* through the matrix at each timestep, generating predictions that are never hard‑wired but emerge from the interaction of stored weights and current input. The two approaches illustrate the spectrum from pure storage to pure flow, but the reality of contemporary AI sits somewhere in the middle.

That middle ground is captured by the **metabolism metaphor**. Just as living organisms ingest nutrients, transform them through metabolic pathways, and excrete waste, an intelligent system ingests raw data, transforms it via computational pathways, and emits behaviour or predictions. The “nutrient” is the information that enters the system; the “circulation” is the algorithmic process that moves that information through layers of representation. This metaphor does more than provide a vivid image—it aligns with the book’s overarching thesis that intelligence is the *metabolism of knowledge* across systems, not a static repository.

Hybrid architectures make the inseparability of storage and flow explicit. Neural‑symbolic systems, for example, embed symbolic rules within a neural substrate, allowing the system to both *store* declarative knowledge and *flow* activation that respects those constraints. In practice, the weight matrix of the neural component stores statistical tendencies, while the symbolic component supplies hard constraints that guide the flow of activation. The result is a system that can learn from data yet remain interpretable enough to be audited—a direct response to the limitations of purely symbolic or purely connectionist models.

Historically, the field moved from **symbolic AI** (the era of expert systems and logic programming) through a **connectionist resurgence** (the rise of neural networks in the 1980s and their explosion in the 2010s) to today’s **hybrid cognitive architectures**. Each stage can be seen as a refinement of the metabolism metaphor: early systems had abundant “nutrients” (knowledge bases) but weak circulation; connectionist models introduced vigorous circulation but initially lacked explicit nutrients; hybrids now strive for a balanced diet of both.

Viewing AI as an **epistemic machine** reframes its role from a passive mirror of human knowledge to an active producer of new knowledge. When a system updates its weights, it is not merely recalling stored facts; it is *generating* a revised internal model that can, in turn, influence future data collection, hypothesis formation, and even scientific discourse. This productionist view forces us to ask who controls the “nutrients” we feed the machine and who decides how the circulation is regulated—a question that will become central when we move from theory to implementation in the lab.

==== Technical Example

To see the storage‑flow duality in action, consider a simple recurrent neural network (RNN) that predicts the next character in a stream of text. During training, the network adjusts a weight matrix W that captures the statistical regularities of the training corpus—this matrix is the *stored representation*. Once training is complete, the network enters inference mode: each incoming character updates the hidden state hₜ by applying W to the previous state and the current input, thereby *flowing* information forward in time. The hidden state never settles into a fixed symbol; it continuously evolves, embodying the metabolism of knowledge as it processes a new sequence.

The same task can be tackled with a symbolic rule‑based parser that encodes explicit bigram rules (e.g., “‘th’ is often followed by a vowel”). Here, the knowledge is a hand‑crafted table of probabilities, and the computation is a deterministic lookup. The parser excels at handling rare or out‑of‑vocabulary sequences if the rule is present, but it struggles with the fluid ambiguities that the RNN smooths over through its distributed representation. By juxtaposing the two, we see complementary strengths: the symbolic system offers interpretability and guarantees, while the RNN provides robustness through flowing activation.

A hybrid RNN‑rule system can combine these virtues. One design embeds the symbolic bigram constraints as a gating mechanism that modulates the hidden‑state update: if a rule predicts a low‑probability continuation, the gate suppresses the corresponding activation in the RNN’s output distribution. In this configuration, the weight matrix remains the primary storage of statistical knowledge, while the symbolic rules act as *metabolic regulators* that steer the flow of activation toward more plausible outcomes. Visualising the hidden‑state dynamics—e.g., using a t‑SNE plot over time—reveals trajectories that bend and twist whenever a rule intervenes, making the “circulation” of information visibly shaped by stored constraints.

The following minimal Python + PyTorch snippet implements the core of this hybrid system. It defines an RNN cell, loads a pre‑trained weight matrix, and applies a rule‑based mask during inference. The code is deliberately compact so that students can focus on the conceptual interplay rather than boiler‑plate details.

```python
import torch, torch.nn as nn

class HybridRNN(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, hidden_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.embed = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, hidden_dim)
        self.rnn   = nn.RNNCell(hidden_dim, hidden_dim)
        self.out   = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, vocab_size)

    def forward(self, idx, hidden, rule_mask):
        x = self.embed(idx)
        hidden = self.rnn(x, hidden)
        logits = self.out(hidden)
        # Apply rule‑based mask (0 = forbidden, 1 = allowed)
        logits = logits + (rule_mask.log() - rule_mask.log())
        return logits, hidden

# Example usage:

# rule_mask = torch.ones(1, vocab)       # all allowed; replace with real mask

```

This code illustrates how a *stored* weight matrix (learned during training) and a *flow* of hidden states interact, with a symbolic rule mask acting as a metabolic checkpoint.

==== Philosophical Reflection

If we accept the **processor** view of the mind, then *intelligence* becomes a question of *how* information is transformed rather than *what* information is possessed. This reorientation foregrounds the dynamics of cognition—learning, inference, adaptation—over the static catalogues of concepts that earlier theories privileged. It also invites us to reconsider long‑standing debates about the nature of mental representation: are symbols merely placeholders, or are they the *vehicles* through which transformation occurs?

The computational metaphor, however, risks eclipsing the embodied, affective, and environmental dimensions that many cognitive scientists argue are essential to cognition. A purely algorithmic description may capture the flow of symbols but can miss the way bodily states, emotions, and situated interactions shape that flow. Embodied cognition scholars would point out that the “nutrients” feeding an AI system are not just data points but also the *contexts* in which those data arise. Ignoring this context can lead to systems that process information efficiently yet remain blind to the lived realities they are meant to serve.

From an epistemological standpoint, stored representations are never neutral; they are *theory‑laden* snapshots of the world. When a process accesses a representation, it can *reshape* the underlying theory by reinforcing certain patterns and suppressing others—a feedback loop reminiscent of Kuhnian paradigm shifts. The metabolism metaphor makes this feedback explicit: just as metabolic pathways can be up‑regulated or down‑regulated in response to physiological demands, AI systems can modify their internal weights (the stored knowledge) in response to the demands of ongoing computation. This raises profound questions about autonomy: can a system become sufficiently self‑modifying that it effectively *rewrites* its own epistemic foundations without external supervision?

Viewing AI as *infrastructure* foregrounds power dynamics embedded in the design of both nutrients and circulation. Designers decide which datasets are ingested, which loss functions guide weight updates, and which symbolic constraints are imposed. Those choices encode values, biases, and political priorities. When an AI system is deployed as a decision‑making substrate—say, in hiring or policing—the metabolic flow of information becomes a conduit for those embedded values, amplifying or attenuating them at scale. Recognising AI as infrastructure thus obliges us to ask: who controls the “diet” of the machine, and who monitors the health of its circulatory system?

Finally, the cognitive turn blurs the classic *mind‑body* split. In hybrid architectures, hardware (the physical substrate), software (the algorithms), and environment (the data stream) co‑constitute the metabolic process. The boundaries dissolve, suggesting a fluid continuum where changes in any one component ripple through the entire system. This perspective prepares us for the next lecture on “Intelligence as Metabolism,” where we will explore how systemic emergence arises when multiple metabolic loops intertwine.
