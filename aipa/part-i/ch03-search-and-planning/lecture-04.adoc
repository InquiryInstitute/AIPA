=== 3.4: Adversarial Search — Game Trees
:leveloffset: 2

Imagine you are facing the last move in a tic‑tac‑toe match.  
The board shows two X’s in a row and your opponent threatens an immediate win.  
What single move guarantees at least a draw, no matter how cleverly your opponent replies?  
This tiny dilemma already contains the whole of *adversarial search*: a question about which *future* we can force when another intelligent agent is actively trying to thwart us.

*Adversarial search: the opponent is part of the state space.*

==== Example Prompts

* "What's the best move in this game?" — _Minimax: alternate turns, opponent minimizes our utility_
* "Evaluate this board position." — _Evaluation function: static score; α‑β prunes branches_
* "Find the optimal play against a rational opponent." — _Game tree: state space includes opponent's moves_

==== Conceptual Core

**From a single question to a full tree.**  
When we ask “If I could see every possible continuation, which move should I pick?” we are implicitly constructing a *game tree*: a branching representation of every legal state that can arise from the current position.  The root is the position we face now; each edge is a legal move; the leaves are terminal outcomes (win, loss, draw).  The tree grows outward until the game ends or we decide to stop because the horizon is too far.

**Game‑tree anatomy and the minimax intuition.**  
Each node stores a complete description of the board *and* whose turn it is.  Because the two players alternate, the tree naturally splits into **MAX** nodes (our turn) and **MIN** nodes (the opponent’s turn).  This alternating structure lets us treat the opponent as another part of the search space rather than as a static environment.  The *minimax* principle then answers the question: *“If it is my turn, which child gives me the best worst‑case result?”*  Formally:

* Terminal nodes return a **utility** (win = **+1**, loss = **‑1**, draw = **0**).  
* At a **MAX** node we take the **maximum** of the children’s values – we assume we will choose the best move.  
* At a **MIN** node we take the **minimum** – we assume the opponent will choose the move that hurts us most.  

The child of the root with the highest (for MAX) or lowest (for MIN) minimax value is the optimal move.

**Mini‑case study: a concrete walk‑through.**  
Return to the opening board from the hook:

```
 X |   |  
---+---+---
   | O |  
---+---+---
   |   |  
```

Suppose it is **X**’s turn (a **MAX** node).  X can play in any of the six empty squares.  If X chooses the centre‑right cell, the board becomes:

```
 X |   | X
---+---+---
   | O |  
---+---+---
   |   |  
```

Now it is **O**’s turn (a **MIN** node).  O can block the immediate threat by playing bottom‑right, yielding a leaf with utility **‑1** (X loses).  If O instead plays elsewhere, X will complete a three‑in‑a‑row on the next move, giving utility **+1**.  The MIN node therefore returns the *minimum* of its children, **‑1**, because O will choose the blocking move.  Repeating this reasoning for every initial X move produces a table of minimax values: the centre‑left and centre‑right moves each return **0** (draw), while any other move returns **‑1** (loss).  Consequently, the optimal strategy for X is to play either centre‑left or centre‑right – exactly the move that guarantees a draw in the opening scenario.

**α‑β pruning: discarding the irrelevant before we even look.**  
The minimax recursion is exhaustive: it evaluates every leaf in the tree.  In practice this is impossible for anything beyond the smallest games.  α‑β pruning introduces two *global* bounds that let us stop exploring branches that cannot affect the final decision:

* **α (alpha)** – the best value that **MAX** can already guarantee.  
* **β (beta)** – the best value that **MIN** can already guarantee.  

During a depth‑first walk, whenever we discover that **α ≥ β** at a node, we know that the opponent can force a result at least as good (for them) as the one we have already found, so the remaining siblings are **pruned**.  *Think‑pair‑share*: hand out a tiny three‑level tree on a slip of paper and ask students to identify which leaf will be pruned before it is evaluated.  The answer illustrates the dramatic reduction in work – often cutting the effective branching factor by half or more.

**Depth limits, the horizon effect, and design choices.**  
Real games such as chess or Go are astronomically larger than tic‑tac‑toe, so we impose a depth limit *d* and replace the true utility at the frontier with a *heuristic evaluation function*.  Propagating these estimates upward yields a *bounded* minimax value, but it also creates a **horizon effect**: threats that lie just beyond depth *d* remain invisible, potentially leading to short‑sighted blunders.  Designing a good evaluation function therefore becomes a central engineering task – it encodes domain expertise (material balance in chess, territory in Go, two‑in‑a‑row threats in tic‑tac‑toe).  Later chapters will show how learning algorithms can *learn* such heuristics from data, turning the hand‑crafted evaluation into a trainable model.

===== Key Points

* Game tree: nodes = states (board + player), edges = legal moves, alternating **MAX**/**MIN**  
* Minimax recursion: terminal → utility; **MAX** → max of children; **MIN** → min of children  
* Optimal move = child of root with best minimax value for the player to act  
* Mini‑case study demonstrates concrete minimax values on a tic‑tac‑toe board  
* α‑β pruning keeps global α (best for **MAX**) and β (best for **MIN**); prune when α ≥ β  
* Pruning can be identified before leaf evaluation – active‑learning moment for students  
* Depth‑limited search replaces terminal utilities with heuristic evaluation, introducing the horizon effect  
* Evaluation functions embed expert knowledge; later chapters cover learning them automatically  
* Rational‑opponent baseline can be enriched with bounded or probabilistic models  

==== Technical Example

**Plain minimax on tic‑tac‑toe.**  
A board is stored as a nine‑character string, e.g. `"XOX  O   "`.  The terminal test scans rows, columns, and diagonals for three‑in‑a‑row or a full board, returning **+1** for a win, **‑1** for a loss, and **0** for a draw.  A naïve recursive minimax explores every legal move, calls itself on the resulting child state, and finally returns the minimax value of the root.  The pseudocode is intentionally simple so that the underlying idea – “choose the move that maximises the worst‑case outcome” – is crystal clear.

**Adding α‑β pruning with a step‑by‑step trace.**  
The recursive function now receives two extra arguments, `alpha` (initially ‑∞) and `beta` (initially +∞).  At a **MAX** node we update `alpha` with the best value seen so far; at a **MIN** node we update `beta`.  After each child we test `if alpha >= beta` – when true we abort the loop, discarding the remaining siblings.  In the depth‑2 tree of Figure 3.4 the left **MIN** node yields a child value **3**, raising **α** to **3**.  When the algorithm reaches the right **MIN** node, **β** is already **2**, so the condition **α ≥ β** holds and the entire right subtree is pruned without evaluating its leaves.  A short print‑out trace (shown in the lab starter code) lets students see the exact moment the cut‑off occurs.

**Scaling up: depth limits, iterative deepening, and evaluation functions.**  
Running plain minimax on tic‑tac‑toe visits roughly **9 000** nodes, whereas the α‑β version visits only **2 500**, a 72 % reduction.  For larger domains (e.g., Connect Four) we combine three techniques:

1. **Depth limit** – restrict the search to *d* = 6 plies.  
2. **Heuristic evaluation** – count open‑ended “four‑in‑a‑row” patterns, assign weights to centre control, etc.  
3. **Iterative deepening** – repeatedly run depth‑limited searches with increasing *d*; each iteration re‑uses the move ordering discovered previously, which dramatically improves α‑β effectiveness under a strict time budget.  

These tricks let the same algorithm explore far deeper positions than a naïve search ever could, and they set the stage for the learning‑based evaluation functions introduced in Chapter 7.

===== Key Points

* Tic‑tac‑toe state = 9‑character string; terminal test returns utility (**+1**/‑1/**0**)  
* Plain minimax recursively explores the full tree  
* α‑β adds `alpha`/`beta` parameters; prune when `alpha >= beta`  
* Trace on Figure 3.4 shows **α = 3** after left subtree, causing right subtree to be cut off  
* Node count: ~9 000 (plain) vs. ~2 500 (α‑β) on tic‑tac‑toe  
* Depth limit + heuristic evaluation enable search in larger games  
* Iterative deepening provides anytime results and respects time limits  
* Pruning dramatically reduces the effective branching factor, allowing deeper look‑ahead  

==== Philosophical Reflection

**Rational versus bounded rational opponents.**  
Minimax gives us a worst‑case guarantee: if we can force at least a draw against a perfect opponent, we will never lose.  Yet real opponents are limited—they make mistakes, have incomplete information, and may not always choose the utility‑minimising move.  In security‑games, for instance, an attacker may lack the computational resources to evaluate every possible intrusion path, leading to *bounded‑rational* behaviour.  Modeling this uncertainty replaces the simple **min** operator with an *expected‑value* calculation or a stochastic policy, turning the deterministic game tree into a probabilistic decision process.

**Evaluation functions as encoded expertise.**  
In complex games the search algorithm is only as good as the heuristic that judges non‑terminal positions.  Designing a strong evaluation function is an act of knowledge engineering: chess programmers count material, piece activity, and king safety; Go programmers estimate territory and influence; even in tic‑tac‑toe a simple count of two‑in‑a‑row threats can dramatically improve play.  This split—*search* versus *knowledge*—mirrors the broader AI divide between combinatorial optimisation and statistical learning.  Later chapters will show how deep neural networks can *learn* evaluation functions from millions of self‑play games, effectively automating the expert‑knowledge step.

**Ethical and multi‑agent horizons.**  
When adversarial search is applied to security, finance, or autonomous systems, the assumption of a perfectly rational adversary can lead to over‑defensive designs (wasting resources) or, conversely, to dangerous under‑estimation of malicious intent.  Incorporating uncertainty about opponent motives, or allowing for partially aligned goals, pushes us toward the broader field of *multi‑agent reasoning* explored in Chapter 8.  Understanding the limits of minimax therefore prepares us to build agents that negotiate, cooperate, or compete under realistic constraints, and to recognise when a game‑theoretic model must be enriched with learning, communication, or ethical safeguards.

===== Key Points

* Minimax provides a worst‑case baseline against a perfect opponent  
* Bounded‑rational or probabilistic opponent models capture realistic behaviour  
* Evaluation functions embed expert knowledge and shape search outcomes  
* Over‑reliance on perfect rationality can produce ethically questionable or inefficient strategies  
* Minimax is a stepping stone toward richer multi‑agent reasoning (Chapter 8)
