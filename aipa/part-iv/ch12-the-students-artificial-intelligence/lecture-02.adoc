==== Conceptual Core

Imagine a municipal government that commissions an AI system to predict “high‑risk neighborhoods” for future crime‑prevention patrols. Six months later the police chief discovers that the model is flagging affluent districts that already have low crime rates, while the neighborhoods that truly need resources are absent from the alerts. The code runs flawlessly; the failure lies not in the algorithm but in the *problem* that was asked of it. The question “Where should we deploy patrols?” was never examined, and the stakeholders who defined it—city planners, community activists, and the police department—were never brought into a shared conversation. This tension sets the stage for every AI project: before any line of code is written, we must agree on *what* we are trying to solve.

The first dimension of a well‑crafted problem is **Scope**. Scope answers three practical questions: *What will the system do?* *What will it not do?* and *What constraints bound it?* A narrow scope—“predict the probability of a burglary at a residential address in the next 24 hours” —is easier to evaluate than a vague one—“improve public safety.” Below is a concrete illustration for our municipal case:

[options="header"]
|===
| In‑Scope | Out‑of‑Scope

| Predict burglary risk for residential addresses  
| Predict overall “crime hot‑spots” without temporal granularity

| Use only police‑report data (no social‑media scraping)  
| Incorporate private surveillance footage

| Output a geospatial heat‑map that can be exported to the city’s GIS tool  
| Provide real‑time alerts to individual officers on their handheld devices
|===

By spelling out in‑scope and out‑of‑scope items, the team creates a contract that later stakeholders can audit. Scope also encodes resource limits—data availability, compute budget, and regulatory compliance. When scope is left implicit, teams often fall prey to “scope creep,” expanding the problem until it becomes unmanageable.

The second dimension is **Users**. Users are not a monolithic group; they are a constellation of personas, each with distinct goals, expertise, and power. In our example, primary users might be patrol supervisors, secondary users could include community liaison officers, city council members, and the residents themselves. Mapping these personas—through stakeholder‑analysis matrices or simple persona cards—helps the team ask targeted research questions: “What decision does a patrol supervisor need to make each morning?” versus “What accountability does a council member require?” A quick *power‑map* (high‑influence/low‑influence vs. high‑interest/low‑interest) makes explicit which voices can shape the problem and which may be marginalized.

The third dimension is **Value Proposition**. Why does solving this problem matter? Value can be expressed in quantitative terms (e.g., “reduce average response time by 15 %”) or qualitative terms (e.g., “increase community trust in policing”). Articulating a clear metric forces the team to confront trade‑offs early: a model that improves detection accuracy by 2 % may not be worth the additional privacy risk it introduces. Moreover, a well‑stated value proposition provides a yardstick for later **problem‑solution fit** checks: does the proposed AI actually deliver the promised benefit, or does it merely shift effort elsewhere?

All three dimensions—Scope, Users, and Value—must be negotiated *with* stakeholders, not imposed *on* them. This negotiation is a form of **political design**: choosing which users’ needs are prioritized, which constraints are accepted, and which outcomes are deemed valuable. Validation is the mechanism that makes this negotiation visible and accountable. By presenting a draft problem definition to a stakeholder panel, the team *asks* stakeholders to sign off, turning alignment into a concrete commitment. The resulting **validation log**, interview transcripts, and a one‑page problem‑definition brief become traceable artifacts that can be referenced in future audits, impact assessments, or post‑mortems.

When the artifact is in place, the team can answer the hard question: “Did we solve the right problem?” If the answer is “no,” the artifact points directly to the dimensions that need revision—scope, users, or value. In this way, a disciplined problem‑definition process turns an ambiguous, politically charged question into a concrete, testable contract that guides the entire AI lifecycle. Moreover, a well‑scoped problem can yield tangible success: a neighboring city that applied the same three‑dimension framework reduced its average emergency‑response time from 12 minutes to 9 minutes within six months, while also reporting a measurable rise in resident‑perceived safety.

===== Key Points

* Scope: explicit in‑scope / out‑of‑scope list, constraints, and guard against scope creep  
* Users: persona mapping, stakeholder power‑map, primary vs. secondary users, research methods  
* Value Proposition: quantitative / qualitative metrics, success criteria, early trade‑off analysis  
* Problem definition is a negotiated, political act—not a neutral discovery  
* Validation turns alignment into a signed commitment and creates an audit‑ready artifact  
* Validation artifacts (brief, transcripts, log) support future accountability and remediation  
* Early agreement on problem reduces downstream technical waste and ethical risk  
* Success vignette: a city that used the framework cut response time by 15 % and boosted trust  

==== Technical Example

*Gather & Structure Requirements (Steps 1 & 2)*  
Start with the one‑page template shown below. In the left column list **Scope** items as bullet points (e.g., “Predict burglary risk for residential addresses — exclude commercial properties”). In the right column sketch **User Personas** (e.g., “Patrol Supervisor — needs daily risk heat‑maps”). Below the table write a concise **Value Proposition** paragraph (≈ 80 words) that names a measurable outcome (“reduce average response time from 12 minutes to 9 minutes within six months”). Save the document as `ProblemDefinition.pdf`.

[options="header"]
|===
| Scope | Users

| *Predict burglary risk for residential addresses*  
| *Patrol Supervisor* – needs daily risk heat‑maps  

| *Exclude commercial properties and incidents without police reports*  
| *Community Liaison* – wants transparent risk explanations  

| *Exportable to city GIS*  
| *City Planner* – requires budget‑justification data
|===

*Synthesise Findings and Validate (Steps 3 & 4)*  
Conduct three semi‑structured interviews (one primary user, two secondary users). Record audio, take notes, and perform a quick coding pass: label each excerpt as “pain point,” “work‑around,” or “desired outcome.” Populate a three‑row table—**Pain Point**, **Current Work‑around**, **Desired Outcome**—with the coded data. For example, a supervisor may say, “I spend 30 minutes each morning reconciling spreadsheets,” which you code as a *pain point* (time‑consuming manual reconciliation).  

Next, host a 30‑minute validation session with all interviewees plus a senior city official. Present the drafted one‑page problem definition and the pain‑point table. Ask two concrete questions:  

1. “Does this capture the problem you experience?”  
2. “If we built a system that meets these criteria, would you adopt it?”  

Capture each response in a **validation log** (simple spreadsheet with columns: *Stakeholder*, *Question*, *Response*, *Commitment*). If any stakeholder objects or raises a new concern, iterate: adjust scope, refine personas, or revise the value metric, then repeat the validation step until at least 80 % of participants affirm the definition.

By the end of this three‑day sprint the team will have a **validated problem brief** that serves both as a design contract and an audit trail. The brief, interview transcripts, synthesis table, and validation log will be submitted as Lab 1 deliverables.

===== Key Points

* One‑page template (Scope | Users) plus concise Value Proposition  
* Conduct 2–3 semi‑structured interviews (audio + notes)  
* Code excerpts into Pain‑Point / Work‑around / Desired‑Outcome table  
* Synthesize findings to revise scope and personas  
* Run a 30‑minute validation session with a senior decision‑maker  
* Record answers in a validation log (Stakeholder, Question, Response, Commitment)  
* Iterate until ≥ 80 % stakeholder affirmation  
* Submit brief, transcripts, synthesis table, and log for Lab 1 grading  

==== Philosophical Reflection

The claim that “problems are social constructs” reframes problem definition from a neutral discovery to an epistemic act. When a team declares “the problem is X,” it is making a claim about reality that must be justified to a community of interlocutors. This claim is performative: it allocates resources, distributes risk, and sets the linguistic frame for all future discussions. In our municipal AI case, the initial framing—“identify high‑risk neighborhoods”—privileged a policing‑centric view of safety and sidelined community‑led notions of well‑being. By foregrounding stakeholder negotiation, we expose the *contingent* nature of the problem and open space for alternative framings such as “support community‑driven safety initiatives.”

Power asymmetries sharpen the ethical stakes. Front‑line officers, minority residents, and budget‑constrained planners rarely have equal voice in the definition phase. If the problem is framed solely around operational efficiency for patrol supervisors, the resulting AI may reinforce existing surveillance practices and exacerbate inequities. Conversely, explicitly elevating community liaison officers to primary users can shift the value proposition toward transparency and trust‑building, even if it reduces short‑term efficiency gains. Recognizing these trade‑offs forces designers to confront ethical dilemmas early, rather than retroactively after deployment.

Documentation of validation is more than bureaucratic record‑keeping; it is a *political safeguard*. By logging who was consulted, what questions were asked, and what commitments were made, the team creates a public ledger that can be audited by regulators, journalists, or affected citizens. This ledger makes it harder for later stakeholders to claim that the problem was “objectively” defined without human input. Moreover, it provides a mechanism for *responsible withdrawal*: if post‑deployment evidence shows that the original framing caused harm, the same documentation guides a systematic re‑definition process. In this way, validation becomes a living contract that persists throughout the AI system’s lifecycle, embodying the post‑modern principle that knowledge—and the problems it produces—are always provisional and contestable.

===== Key Points

* Problems are epistemic claims that require community justification  
* Framing shapes resource allocation, risk distribution, and future discourse  
* Power asymmetries determine whose needs are encoded in the problem definition  
* Including marginalized voices can shift value toward equity and trust  
* Validation logs act as political safeguards and audit trails  
* Documentation enables systematic re‑definition and responsible withdrawal  
* Treating problem construction as provisional aligns with post‑modern AI ethics
