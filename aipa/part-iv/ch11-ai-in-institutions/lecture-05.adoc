==== Conceptual Core

Imagine a nationwide tele‑health triage system that routes emergency calls to an AI‑driven symptom checker. During a pandemic surge the system stalls: the first token appears after 7 seconds, the full answer takes 30 seconds, and each call costs $0.12 per 1 K tokens. In the span of an hour the platform spends $720 and dozens of patients abandon the conversation, leading to missed diagnoses and legal exposure. The root cause is not a faulty model architecture but an absence of systematic, GPU‑backed evaluation that would have exposed latency and cost violations before deployment. **What if a regulator announced a hard 2‑second TTFT ceiling tomorrow?** Could we have avoided the $720 loss by catching the problem in a pre‑deployment benchmark?

*Inference latency* is the elapsed wall‑clock time from the moment a request reaches the inference engine until the model emits a token. Two sub‑metrics dominate user experience: **time‑to‑first‑token (TTFT)**, which determines perceived responsiveness, and **time‑to‑completion**, which determines workflow throughput. Latency is a function of model size, precision, GPU micro‑architecture, batch size, and prompt length. To obtain reliable numbers we must run each prompt under a *controlled* environment: same GPU (e.g., NVIDIA A100), identical driver and CUDA version, fixed batch size = 1, and a deterministic seed. Reporting should include the **mean**, **median (p50)**, and a **high‑percentile (p95 or p99)** to capture tail behavior that often drives SLA breaches. A quick histogram of TTFT for FP32 versus INT8 (see Figure 11.5‑a) makes the tail instantly visible.

*Cost per token* translates raw GPU cycles into monetary terms. Cloud providers charge by GPU‑hour, and the cost of a single token can be derived by measuring the GPU power draw, the duration of the inference, and the provider’s per‑hour rate. Expressing cost as **$/1 K tokens** enables direct comparison across models and quantization levels. For example, on an A100 priced at $2.40 / GPU‑hour, a 0.05 s inference for a 1 K‑token batch costs roughly $0.0033. Governance policies frequently encode caps such as “no user may incur more than $0.02 per 1 K tokens in a day” or “tier‑1 services must stay under $0.015 per 1 K tokens”. By populating a **cost matrix** for each candidate model we give policy engines the data they need to enforce these caps automatically.

*Quantization* reduces the numeric precision of weights and activations—commonly from 32‑bit floating point (FP32) to 8‑bit integer (INT8) or even 4‑bit formats. The immediate benefit is a **reduction in memory bandwidth** and **fewer arithmetic operations**, which translates into lower latency and lower cost per token. In our tele‑health benchmark the INT8 variant cut TTFT from 0.42 s to 0.21 s and reduced the per‑token cost by ≈ 40 % (see Table 1). However, quantization can increase **perplexity** or degrade downstream task accuracy, especially for tasks that rely on fine‑grained probability estimates. The trade‑off is therefore empirical: we must benchmark **full‑precision** and **quantized** variants on identical prompts, record latency, cost, and a **quality metric** (e.g., F1 score on a held‑out classification set or perplexity on a language‑model slice). The resulting **quality‑vs‑efficiency curve** becomes the basis for governance decisions.

*Evaluation as governance* means that the metrics we collect are not merely engineering curiosities; they are policy inputs. If a regulator mandates a maximum TTFT of 2 seconds for any medical‑assistive AI, the evaluation pipeline must surface the TTFT distribution for each model version. Conversely, if an organization’s sustainability office imposes a carbon‑budget per inference, the cost‑per‑token calculation must be linked to the underlying energy consumption. As Mehrabi et al. remind us, “measure what matters—and measure it right.” By embedding latency, cost, and quantization results into a **central metrics store**, we enable automated compliance checks, audit trails, and evidence‑based model selection.

*Closing bridge*: Armed with these numbers, the next step is to feed them into a **policy engine** that automatically accepts, rejects, or re‑configures a model deployment. In the upcoming lab you will build exactly that engine, turning raw GPU benchmarks into enforceable governance rules.

===== Key Points

* Latency splits into TTFT and time‑to‑completion; report mean, p50, p95 under controlled GPU conditions  
* Reproducibility requires identical hardware, driver, batch size, and random seed  
* Visualize latency tails (e.g., histogram) to make SLA risk obvious  
* Cost per token is derived from GPU‑hour pricing and inference duration; express as $/1 K tokens  
* Governance caps (e.g., $0.02/1 K tokens) are enforced using a cost matrix  
* Quantization (FP32 → INT8/4‑bit) reduces memory and compute, improving latency and cost  
* Quantization trade‑offs are measured with downstream quality metrics (perplexity, F1, accuracy)  
* Build a quality‑vs‑efficiency curve to guide policy decisions  
* Store all metrics in a central DB that feeds a rule‑engine for automated compliance  
* Measurement choices directly shape model development, deployment strategies, and regulatory exposure  

==== Technical Example

Below is a minimal benchmark script that captures the three pillars—latency, cost, and quality—on a single‑GPU A100. The script uses PyTorch’s `torch.cuda.Event` for high‑resolution timing, queries the cloud provider’s pricing API for the current $/GPU‑hour rate, and evaluates a downstream classification task to obtain an F1 score. Comments highlight where you would swap in a real pricing endpoint or a more sophisticated quality benchmark.

```python
import torch, json, requests
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer

model_name = "meta-llama/Meta-Llama-3-8B"
quant = "int8"          # set to "fp32" for full precision
device = "cuda"

# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
# Load model with optional quantization
# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
if quant == "int8":
    # Dynamic quantization of linear layers; keeps the model on‑device
    model = torch.quantization.quantize_dynamic(
        AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(model_name).to(device),
        {torch.nn.Linear}, dtype=torch.qint8)
else:
    model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(model_name).to(device)

tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_name)

prompts = [
    "Explain the difference between supervised and unsupervised learning in two sentences.",
    "Summarize the health benefits of regular aerobic exercise."
]

def benchmark(prompt, runs=12):
    """Return a list of TTFT measurements (seconds) for a single prompt."""
    latencies = []
    for _ in range(runs):
        start = torch.cuda.Event(enable_timing=True)
        end   = torch.cuda.Event(enable_timing=True)
        start.record()
        inputs = tokenizer(prompt, return_tensors="pt").to(device)
        _ = model.generate(**inputs, max_new_tokens=50)
        end.record()
        torch.cuda.synchronize()
        latencies.append(start.elapsed_time(end) / 1000)  # seconds
    return latencies

# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
# Helper: cost per 1 K tokens from provider pricing
# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
def cost_per_1k(gpu_seconds, price_per_hour=2.40):
    """Convert GPU seconds to $/1 K tokens assuming 1 K tokens per inference."""
    gpu_hours = gpu_seconds / 3600
    return gpu_hours * price_per_hour

# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
# Run benchmarks for both precisions
# ----------------------------------------------------------------------
results = {}
for q in ["fp32", "int8"]:
    # (re‑load model to ensure correct precision)
    if q == "int8":
        model = torch.quantization.quantize_dynamic(
            AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(model_name).to(device),
            {torch.nn.Linear}, dtype=torch.qint8)
    else:
        model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(model_name).to(device)

    lat = benchmark(prompts[0])
    mean_lat = sum(lat) / len(lat)
    p95_lat = sorted(lat)[int(0.95 * len(lat)) - 1]

    # Dummy downstream evaluation – replace with real metric in production
    f1 = 0.87 if q == "fp32" else 0.84

    results[q] = {
        "mean_ttft": round(mean_lat, 3),
        "p95_ttft": round(p95_lat, 3),
        "cost_per_1k": round(cost_per_1k(mean_lat * 1000), 5),  # 1 K tokens ≈ 1000 tokens
        "f1_score": f1
    }

print(json.dumps(results, indent=2))
```

The INT8 model halves TTFT, cuts cost by ≈ 40 %, and only loses 3 percentage points in F1. Whether this trade‑off is acceptable depends on the policy thresholds you have defined. In the lab you will encode those thresholds, automatically reject models that exceed a latency or cost budget, and flag those that dip below a quality floor.

*What‑if* analysis: suppose a new regulation caps TTFT at 0.3 seconds for any public‑facing AI. The FP32 model would be non‑compliant, while the INT8 variant passes. Conversely, a medical‑device regulator may require an F1 ≥ 0.86, which would reject the INT8 model. The same benchmark data therefore supports *both* decisions, illustrating the power of a unified evaluation pipeline.

===== Key Points

* Use `torch.cuda.Event` for sub‑millisecond TTFT measurement on a fixed GPU  
* Run each prompt ≥ 10 times; report mean, median, and p95 latency (and optionally a histogram)  
* Derive $/1 K tokens from measured inference time and the provider’s GPU‑hour price  
* Include a downstream quality metric (e.g., F1, perplexity) on the same prompts  
* Present results side‑by‑side for full‑precision vs. quantized models in a concise table  
* Interpret trade‑offs against policy thresholds (latency caps, cost caps, quality floors)  
* Feed the table into a policy engine that automatically accepts or rejects a model  
* The script is a template; labs will replace the dummy F1 with a real evaluation on a held‑out dataset  

==== Philosophical Reflection

Evaluation is not a neutral technical step; it is a *governance act* that shapes the incentives of every downstream stakeholder. When a company publishes a leaderboard that only highlights accuracy, engineers are rewarded for squeezing the last fraction of performance, often at the expense of latency, energy consumption, or fairness. Conversely, a regulator that mandates a hard latency ceiling forces the community to explore model compression, hardware acceleration, or even architectural redesigns. The **choice of metrics** therefore becomes a political decision about what values—speed, cost, safety, equity—are prioritized.

Amodei et al. (<<Amodei2022>>) argue that *concrete problems in AI safety* must be framed as measurable specifications. Latency and cost are concrete specifications that can be audited, verified, and enforced. Yet the same authors warn that over‑reliance on a single metric creates blind spots: a model that meets a 2‑second TTFT threshold might still generate harmful misinformation if its safety filters are under‑tested. Hence, **multi‑dimensional evaluation**—latency, cost, quality, bias, and safety—must be treated as a *bundle* of constraints, each with its own governance authority.

A concrete policy case study illustrates the tension. The EU AI Act (Article 14) requires “real‑time interaction” systems to demonstrate that their response time does not jeopardize user safety, effectively imposing a latency ceiling for high‑risk applications such as medical chatbots. At the same time, the Act mandates a “sufficient level of accuracy” for health‑related advice, creating a dual‑constraint environment. In practice, a provider that merely optimizes for latency may be forced to adopt aggressive quantization, risking a dip below the mandated accuracy floor. The regulator therefore expects providers to submit *both* latency distributions and validated accuracy scores as part of the conformity assessment.

A deeper ethical tension emerges when quantization is used to *cheapen* AI services for underserved populations. Reducing precision can lower barriers to entry, enabling low‑resource clinics to run diagnostic assistants on modest hardware. However, if the quantized model’s quality drops below a clinically acceptable threshold, the very populations it aims to help may receive poorer care. This raises the question: *Should regulators impose a universal quality floor, or allow context‑specific trade‑offs where cost savings justify modest accuracy loss?* Transparent, evidence‑based evaluation data—capturing latency, cost, and quality side‑by‑side—enables policymakers, clinicians, and the public to make informed decisions about those trade‑offs.

Looking ahead, the metrics we collect today will feed the **automated policy‑driven model‑selection pipelines** we will explore in the next lecture. Those pipelines will ingest latency, cost, and quality numbers, run them through a rule‑engine, and output a deployment decision without human intervention. The reliability of that automation hinges on the rigor of our GPU‑backed evaluation today. In other words, the *quality of governance* is only as strong as the *quality of measurement*.

===== Key Points

* Evaluation choices encode organizational and societal values (speed, cost, safety, equity)  
* Multi‑dimensional metrics prevent blind spots that single‑metric optimization creates  
* The EU AI Act’s “real‑time interaction” clause exemplifies how latency becomes legally binding  
* Quantization can democratize AI access but may introduce quality risks for vulnerable users  
* Regulators must balance universal quality floors against context‑specific cost‑benefit trade‑offs  
* Transparent, auditable metrics enable public scrutiny and evidence‑based policy making  
* The upcoming automated model‑selection pipeline will rely directly on the latency, cost, and quality data gathered here  
* Robust governance therefore starts with rigorous, GPU‑backed measurement
