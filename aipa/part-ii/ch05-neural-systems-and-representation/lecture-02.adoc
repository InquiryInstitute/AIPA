=== 5.2: Multilayer Networks and Backpropagation
:leveloffset: 2

Imagine you are programming a small robot arm that must decide whether a visual cue on a screen is a “⊕” or a “⊖”. The first attempt uses a single‑layer perceptron. The robot repeatedly mis‑classifies the “⊖” pattern, flipping its arm in the wrong direction. When you plot the decision boundary you see a straight line that can never separate the two shapes—XOR is simply *non‑linear*. You try to “fix” the problem by adding a second linear layer, hoping that the extra depth will give the robot more flexibility. The robot’s performance does not improve; the decision boundary is still a single line. *Why does stacking linear maps not help?* This failure becomes the hook for today’s story: depth alone is insufficient, we need **non‑linearity** and a systematic way to tell each weight how much it contributed to the error.

The missing ingredient is an activation function that breaks the linear chain. After each affine transformation we insert a pointwise non‑linear map—ReLU, sigmoid, tanh, or softmax. ReLU ( max(0, x) ) passes positive signals unchanged and zeroes out negatives; it is cheap to compute and keeps gradients alive for active units. Sigmoid squashes values into (0, 1), making it natural for probabilistic outputs but it can saturate, causing gradients to vanish. Tanh behaves like a centered sigmoid, and softmax turns a vector of scores into a probability distribution, usually reserved for the final classification layer. By interleaving linear maps with these non‑linearities the network can carve out piecewise‑linear or curved decision surfaces that finally separate the XOR patterns. The robot’s arm now swings correctly after just one hidden layer equipped with ReLU.

The breakthrough that turned this intuition into a practical algorithm arrived in 1986 with the Rumelhart‑Hinton‑Williams paper. They showed that, because every unit is differentiable, we can apply the chain rule *backwards* through the network to compute an exact gradient for **every** weight. This algorithm—*backpropagation*—solves the credit‑assignment problem: each weight receives a signal proportional to how much it contributed to the final loss. The robot’s controller can now adjust every connection in proportion to its responsibility for a mis‑classification, rather than guessing blindly.

A theoretical safety net, the universal approximation theorem, tells us that a single hidden layer with enough neurons and a non‑linear activation can approximate any continuous function on a compact domain. In practice, however, such a shallow network would need millions of parameters, would be difficult to train, and would not reuse low‑level features. Depth gives us **parameter efficiency**: early layers learn generic motifs (edges, simple textures), later layers recombine them into increasingly abstract concepts (corners, object parts, whole objects). This hierarchical reuse is why deep nets outperform wide shallow nets on real‑world perception tasks—our robot can now recognize a “⊕” even when it appears at different scales or orientations because the same low‑level edge detectors are reused throughout the network.

The engine that drives this efficiency is the chain‑rule computation of backpropagation. For a network with \(K\) layers, the error signal at layer \(k\) is  

\[
\delta^{(k)} \;=\; \frac{\partial L}{\partial z^{(k)}} \;=\; \bigl(\mathbf{W}^{(k+1)^\top}\,\delta^{(k+1)}\bigr) \;\odot\; \phi'\!\bigl(z^{(k)}\bigr)
\]

where \(z^{(k)}\) is the pre‑activation vector, \(\phi'\) the derivative of the activation, and \(\odot\) the element‑wise product. The magnitude of \(\delta^{(k)}\) tells us how much each hidden unit contributed to the loss \(L\).  

*Live‑demo note:* In the accompanying notebook we will plot the loss surface for a tiny 2‑layer net and overlay the gradient vector \(\delta^{(1)}\) to see exactly how the robot’s parameters would move in response to an error.

**Numerical illustration** – Consider a 2‑layer network with input \(\mathbf{x}= [0.5,\,-0.2]\), weight matrix  

\[
\mathbf{W}^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & -0.5 \\ 0.3 & 0.9 \end{bmatrix},
\qquad \mathbf{b}^{(1)} = \mathbf{0},
\]

ReLU activation, and output layer \(\mathbf{W}^{(2)} = [1.2,\; -0.7]\), \(b^{(2)} = 0.1\).  
A forward pass yields  

\[
z^{(1)} = \mathbf{W}^{(1)}\mathbf{x}= [0.5,\,-0.38],\quad
a^{(1)} = \text{ReLU}(z^{(1)}) = [0.5,\,0],
\]  

\[
z^{(2)} = \mathbf{W}^{(2)}a^{(1)} + b^{(2)} = 0.7,\quad
\hat y = \sigma(z^{(2)}) = 0.668,
\]  

where \(\sigma\) is the logistic sigmoid. With target \(y=1\) and binary‑cross‑entropy loss, the output‑layer error is \(\delta^{(2)} = \hat y - y = -0.332\). Propagating back,  

\[
\delta^{(1)} = (\mathbf{W}^{(2)^\top}\delta^{(2)}) \odot \phi'(z^{(1)}) 
               = [-0.398,\;0],
\]  

because \(\phi'(z^{(1)}) = [1,\,0]\) for ReLU. The gradients are then  

\[
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W}^{(1)}} = \delta^{(1)}\mathbf{x}^\top,\qquad
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W}^{(2)}} = \delta^{(2)} a^{(1)}^\top.
\]  

This arithmetic makes the abstract chain rule concrete: each weight receives a signed update proportional to the product of its input activation and the downstream error signal.

===== Key Points

* Adding a linear layer to another linear layer does **not** increase expressive power; the composition is still affine.  
* Pointwise non‑linearities (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh, softmax) break the linear chain and enable complex decision boundaries.  
* The 1986 Rumelhart‑Hinton‑Williams paper introduced **backpropagation**, turning credit assignment into a systematic gradient computation.  
* Universal approximation guarantees existence of a shallow solution, but depth yields **parameter efficiency** and hierarchical feature reuse.  
* The backpropagation recurrence \(\delta^{(k)} = (\mathbf{W}^{(k+1)^\top}\delta^{(k+1)}) \odot \phi'(z^{(k)})\) propagates error signals from output to input.  
* A concrete 2‑layer numeric example shows how \(\delta\) is computed and how gradients are assembled.  
* The robot’s failure on XOR illustrates the need for both depth *and* non‑linearity before backprop can succeed.  

==== Technical Example

Implementing a multilayer perceptron (MLP) with two hidden layers follows a clear two‑phase pattern: **forward** to compute predictions and **backward** to compute gradients.  

*Forward pass (single paragraph)* – Given an input \(\mathbf{x}\), we compute for each layer \(k\) the pre‑activation \(z^{(k)} = \mathbf{W}^{(k)}a^{(k-1)} + \mathbf{b}^{(k)}\) and then the activation \(a^{(k)} = \phi\!\bigl(z^{(k)}\bigr)\). For a network with two hidden layers we obtain  

\[
\begin{aligned}
z^{(1)} &= \mathbf{W}^{(1)}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}^{(1)}, & a^{(1)} &= \text{ReLU}(z^{(1)}),\\
z^{(2)} &= \mathbf{W}^{(2)}a^{(1)} + \mathbf{b}^{(2)}, & a^{(2)} &= \text{ReLU}(z^{(2)}),\\
z^{(3)} &= \mathbf{W}^{(3)}a^{(2)} + \mathbf{b}^{(3)}, & \hat{\mathbf{y}} &= \text{Softmax}(z^{(3)}).
\end{aligned}
\]  

All \(z^{(k)}\) and \(a^{(k)}\) are cached because they are needed for the backward pass.

*Backward pass (single paragraph)* – Starting from the loss gradient w.r.t. the softmax output, \(\delta^{(3)} = \hat{\mathbf{y}} - \mathbf{y}\), we propagate the error backward using the chain‑rule recurrence.  

\[
\begin{aligned}
\delta^{(2)} &= (\mathbf{W}^{(3)^\top}\delta^{(3)}) \odot \mathbf{1}_{z^{(2)}>0},\\
\delta^{(1)} &= (\mathbf{W}^{(2)^\top}\delta^{(2)}) \odot \mathbf{1}_{z^{(1)}>0},
\end{aligned}
\]  

where \(\mathbf{1}_{z>0}\) is the ReLU derivative (1 for positive pre‑activations, 0 otherwise). Gradients for each weight matrix follow  

\[
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W}^{(k)}} = \delta^{(k)} a^{(k-1)\top},
\qquad
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{b}^{(k)}} = \delta^{(k)}.
\]  

*Practical checklist (single short paragraph)* – Before training, initialise weights with a distribution matched to the activation (He for ReLU, Xavier for sigmoid). Choose a modest learning rate (e.g., 0.01) and verify the implementation with a finite‑difference **gradient check**: perturb each weight by \(\pm\varepsilon\) (with \(\varepsilon = 10^{-5}\)) and compare the numerical gradient \(\frac{L(w+\varepsilon)-L(w-\varepsilon)}{2\varepsilon}\) to the analytical \(\partial L/\partial w\). Discrepancies indicate bugs that would otherwise corrupt the learning dynamics.

===== Key Points

* Forward pass stores every \(z^{(k)}\) and \(a^{(k)}\) for reuse in backprop.  
* ReLU’s derivative is a binary mask \(\mathbf{1}_{z>0}\); sigmoid’s derivative is \(\sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))\).  
* Backward pass applies the chain‑rule layer‑by‑layer, yielding error signals \(\delta^{(k)}\).  
* Gradients are outer products of \(\delta^{(k)}\) with the previous activation \(a^{(k-1)}\).  
* Finite‑difference gradient checks validate the analytical implementation.  
* Proper weight initialisation (He/Xavier) and learning‑rate selection prevent exploding or vanishing gradients.

==== Philosophical Reflection

Representation learning asks a deceptively simple question: *what do the hidden layers actually encode?* In a vision‑oriented robot, the first hidden layer often discovers edge detectors—simple, oriented gradients that respond to changes in pixel intensity. The second layer combines these edges into motifs such as corners or simple textures. Deeper layers begin to assemble motifs into parts of objects (e.g., a wheel, a handle) and eventually into whole‑object concepts (a screwdriver, a cup). This hierarchy is **emergent**; it is not hard‑coded but discovered through the pressure of minimizing loss on the training data. When we visualise the learned filters of the robot’s first layer, we see familiar Gabor‑like patterns, but the higher‑layer activations remain opaque, resisting straightforward human interpretation.

The opacity creates a philosophical tension. On the one hand, the network behaves as if it has built an internal model of the world—a model that can generalise to new viewpoints or lighting conditions. On the other hand, the “meaning” of a unit is a side‑effect of optimisation, not a deliberately designed symbol. Are we uncovering a computational analogue of human cognition, or merely engineering a statistical device that happens to work? This question matters when we consider accountability: if the robot mis‑classifies a safety‑critical pattern, can we explain *why* a particular hidden unit fired? The answer influences how we evaluate, interpret, and responsibly deploy deep models.

Looking forward, the next practical obstacle is **gradient flow**. Even with ReLU keeping gradients alive in many units, deep networks can still suffer from vanishing or exploding signals, especially when the loss landscape is steep or flat. The upcoming lecture on *training dynamics* will introduce tricks—initialisation schemes, batch normalisation, and residual connections—that preserve the magnitude of \(\delta^{(k)}\) across many layers. Mastering these techniques will allow us to scale the elegant backprop engine from our toy robot to full‑scale perception pipelines, ultimately feeding the `neural_classifier` that will drive the robot’s decision‑making in the real world.

##### Key Points

* Hidden layers form an emergent hierarchy from edges to object concepts.  
* Visualising early‑layer filters offers intuition; deeper representations remain largely opaque.  
* Learned representations arise from loss‑driven optimisation, not explicit design, raising questions about “understanding”.  
* The tension between cognitive‑model interpretation and statistical‑engine performance informs ethical deployment.  
* Gradient‑flow problems (vanishing/exploding) become the next practical hurdle.  
* Upcoming training‑dynamics tricks (initialisation, normalisation, residuals) keep backprop effective in deep nets.  
* Mastery of these tricks will enable the robot’s `neural_classifier` to scale from toy examples to real‑world perception.
