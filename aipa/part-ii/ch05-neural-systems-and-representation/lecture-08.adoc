==== Hook – The Real‑World Tension

You open your email client each morning and are immediately greeted by a mixture of newsletters, work updates, and a handful of suspicious messages that look almost legitimate. The cost of a single successful phishing email can be thousands of dollars, yet the solution cannot rely on a cloud service that constantly streams data to a remote server—privacy regulations, bandwidth limits, and latency concerns forbid it.  

The question that drives today’s lecture is therefore two‑fold:  

* How can we design a model that is **small enough** to ship on a laptop or a mobile device, yet **accurate enough** to flag the majority of malicious emails?  
* Once the model is trained, how do we **export** it into a platform‑agnostic format that can be executed by any runtime, including edge‑optimized inference engines?

We will answer these questions by walking through the complete lifecycle of a lightweight transformer‑based email classifier, from data preprocessing to ONNX export, and we will finish by reflecting on the broader ethical implications of deploying such a system.

---

==== Conceptual Core

The core ideas that underpin a tiny transformer email classifier can be grouped into six thematic pillars. Each pillar answers a question raised in the hook and builds on the previous one, creating a step‑wise narrative that leads naturally to the technical implementation.

. **Why Transformers for Text?**  
   *Self‑attention* lets a model weigh the importance of each token relative to every other token, capturing long‑range dependencies that bag‑of‑words or n‑gram models miss. Even a *small* transformer (few layers, limited hidden size) retains this expressive power, making it ideal for nuanced tasks like phishing detection where subtle phrasing matters.

. **What Does “Small” Mean?**  
   A *small* transformer typically has 2–4 encoder layers, 2–4 attention heads per layer, and a hidden dimension of 128–256. This reduces the parameter count from tens of millions (e.g., BERT‑base) to under one million, dramatically shrinking memory footprint and inference latency.

. **How Do We Prepare Email Data?**  
   Raw emails must be cleaned (HTML stripping, header removal), tokenized (sub‑word tokenizers such as Byte‑Pair Encoding), and padded/truncated to a fixed length (e.g., 128 tokens). Class imbalance is addressed through *weighted* loss functions or *oversampling* of the minority (phishing) class.

. **Which Training Objective Is Appropriate?**  
   *Cross‑entropy* loss paired with a softmax output layer directly optimizes for classification accuracy. When class imbalance is severe, we augment the loss with class weights or focal loss to penalize mis‑classifications of the rare class more heavily.

. **How Do We Export for Portability?**  
   ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange) provides a framework‑agnostic representation of the trained model. Exporting to ONNX decouples the model from the training library (PyTorch, TensorFlow) and enables execution on a wide range of runtimes, including ONNX Runtime, TensorRT, and mobile‑optimized inference engines.

. **What Are the Deployment Constraints?**  
   Edge devices impose strict limits on *memory* (≤ 200 MB), *compute* (≤ 2 GFLOPs), and *power* (battery life). Quantization (e.g., 8‑bit integer) and pruning are common post‑training optimizations that further shrink the model while preserving most of its accuracy.

These six pillars answer the hook’s questions: a tiny transformer can capture the necessary linguistic nuance, and ONNX export makes the model portable to constrained environments.

==== Technical Example

Below we walk through a concrete implementation of the ideas above. The code is deliberately broken into three logical blocks, each accompanied by a short explanatory paragraph and **no more than two** key‑point bullets.

[source,python]
----
# 1️⃣  Imports & configuration
from neural_classifier import create_architecture, train, predict, export
import torch

config = {
    "arch": "transformer_small",   # 2 encoder layers, 4 heads, hidden=128
    "lr": 3e-4,
    "epochs": 10,
    "batch_size": 32,
    "loss": "cross_entropy",
    "weight_decay": 1e-5,
    "class_weights": [0.3, 0.7]    # lower weight for abundant ham class
}
----
*Key Points*  
- Selecting the predefined “tiny” architecture limits parameters to ≈ 0.9 M.  
- Class‑weighting directly counters the typical 70 % ham / 30 % phishing imbalance.

[source,python]
----
# 2️⃣  Model creation, training, and early evaluation
model = create_architecture(config)
train(
    model,
    train_dataset,
    val_dataset,
    config,
    callbacks=[
        {"type": "EarlyStopping", "patience": 3},
        {"type": "LRScheduler", "schedule": "cosine"}
    ]
)

preds = predict(model, test_dataset)
from sklearn.metrics import classification_report
print(classification_report(test_dataset.labels, preds))
----
*Key Points*  
- Early stopping prevents over‑fitting on the modest dataset.  
- The classification report highlights *recall* on the phishing class, the metric most relevant for security‑focused deployments.

[source,python]
----
# 3️⃣  Export, quantization, and a concrete email test
export_path = "models/email_classifier.onnx"
export(model, export_path, input_shape=(1, 128))

# Load the ONNX model with runtime‑optimized settings
import onnxruntime as ort
sess = ort.InferenceSession(export_path, providers=["CPUExecutionProvider"])
# Example of a suspicious email (subject + body)
sample_email = "Subject: Verify your account\nBody: Dear user, please click https://secure-login.example.com to confirm your credentials."
tokens = tokenizer(sample_email, max_length=128, truncation=True, return_tensors="pt")
attention = sess.run(None, {"input_ids": tokens["input_ids"].numpy()})[0]

print("Phishing probability:", attention[0][1])
----
*Key Points*  
- ONNX export decouples the model from PyTorch, enabling execution on any platform.  
- A single‑email inference demonstrates the end‑to‑end workflow and yields a probability that can be thresholded for real‑time blocking.

==== Philosophical Reflection

The technical choices we have made are not value‑neutral. Deploying an email classifier on personal devices raises questions about privacy, bias, and responsibility.

. **Privacy vs. Security**  
   Training on real user emails can improve model robustness, but it also risks exposing sensitive personal information. Techniques such as *federated learning* or *differential privacy* can mitigate this tension by keeping raw data on‑device while still benefiting from collective knowledge.

. **Bias in Training Data**  
   Public spam datasets (e.g., Enron, SpamAssassin) are historically skewed toward English‑language corporate communications. A model trained solely on these corpora may under‑perform on multilingual or informal phishing attempts, inadvertently disadvantaging non‑English speakers.

. **Model Transparency**  
   Transformers are often criticized as “black boxes.” However, the small size of our model makes *attention visualization* feasible. By inspecting attention weights on a real phishing email—e.g., the phrase “click https://secure‑login.example.com”—developers can audit whether the model focuses on suspicious cues or irrelevant filler.

. **Responsibility of Deployment**  
   An inaccurate classifier can cause *false positives* (blocking legitimate emails) or *false negatives* (letting phishing through). Both outcomes have real‑world costs: productivity loss versus security breach. Organizations must therefore define acceptable error thresholds and provide users with override mechanisms.

. **Open‑Source Export and Security**  
   Exporting to ONNX democratizes access to the model, enabling third‑party audits and community‑driven improvements. Yet it also opens the door to malicious actors who might reverse‑engineer the model to craft adversarial emails. Secure distribution channels and model signing become essential safeguards.

These reflections remind us that building a performant classifier is only half the battle; ensuring its ethical, fair, and secure use completes the story.
