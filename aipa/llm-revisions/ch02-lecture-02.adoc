=== 2.3: Data Pipelines and Knowledge Flow

*Data does not "flow" by itself—it is moved, transformed, and contested by actors in a network.*

==== Conceptual Core

Data pipelines are the circulatory system of AI. Raw data is ingested, transformed, stored, and served. Training data flows into model development; inference data flows through the deployed model; outputs may feed back as new training data. The pipeline is not passive infrastructure—it is a site of decisions. What data is included? What is excluded? Who transforms it, and by what rules? Actor‑network theory (ANT) reminds us: data does not move by itself. It is moved, shaped, and contested by actors—humans, scripts, models, regulations—in a network.

Training data produces the model; the model produces outputs; outputs may become training data. The feedback loop is epistemic. A recommendation system recommends; users click; clicks become signals; signals retrain the model. The system does not "reflect" user preferences—it co‑produces them. Knowledge flow asks: who produces training data? Who consumes outputs? Who governs the pipeline? Pipelines are infrastructure in the sense of Chapter 1: they enable other activity, and they are often invisible until they fail. A biased pipeline produces biased models; a leaky pipeline leaks; a broken pipeline breaks everything downstream.

The pipeline stages are not neutral. Raw data carries the biases and exclusions of its sources. Preprocessing encodes choices about normalization, imputation, and feature engineering. Training optimizes an objective—accuracy, revenue, engagement—that may not align with fairness or safety. Each stage is a site of epistemic and political choice. When we audit a system, we audit the pipeline: what flows in, what is transformed, how, and what flows out. The audit tool you build will document this flow.

**Governance** of a pipeline is a crucial, often hidden layer. Version control of data schemas, model registries, and explicit change‑log policies ensure that every transformation can be traced and rolled back. Regulatory compliance (e.g., GDPR, sector‑specific standards) imposes constraints on what may be collected, how it may be stored, and who may access it. Without systematic governance, pipelines become ad‑hoc, making accountability impossible.

**Observability** turns the invisible into the visible. Logging at each stage, metric dashboards for data quality, and automated alerts for drift or failure surface the health of the pipeline. When a bias‑amplifying feedback loop emerges, observability tools flag anomalous patterns, prompting intervention before downstream harm accrues. In this way, pipelines become not only conduits of knowledge but also monitored ecosystems.

===== Key Points

* Data pipelines: ingestion, transformation, storage, serving
* Training data → model → inference → (feedback) → training data
* Data does not flow by itself—actors move, transform, contest
* Feedback loop: outputs become training signals
* Who produces? Who consumes? Who governs?
* Pipelines as infrastructure—invisible until they fail
* ANT: actors (humans, scripts, models, regulations) shape the network
* Epistemic loop: recommendation → click → signal → retrain

Raw data may be incomplete or biased; preprocessing may introduce errors; training may overfit; deployment may face distribution shift. ==== Philosophical Reflection

Data is a trace of practice. What gets logged, what gets labeled, what gets included in a training set—each choice excludes something. The excluded may be the most important: the users who did not click, the cases that were never labeled, the domains that were deemed out of scope. Epistemic circulation in ML systems is not neutral. The pipeline embodies decisions about what counts as data, what counts as signal, and what counts as noise. Dewey's formulation—knowledge as doing, not possession—fits. Knowledge is produced through the pipeline's operation, not stored in a static repository. Moreover, Dewey’s emphasis on “knowledge as doing” foregrounds **responsibility**: the actors who shape the pipeline must be accountable for the epistemic consequences of their choices.

When we audit a system, we audit the pipeline. The inventory (Lab 1) includes data sources and transformations. The traceability design (Lab 2) will log what flows where. The audit report (Lab 3) will surface the epistemic structure: who produces what, who consumes what, and what might be missing. Data pipelines are knowledge‑producing machinery. Treat them as such.

===== Key Points

* Data as trace of practice—exclusions matter
* Epistemic circulation: what counts as data, signal, noise
* Knowledge produced through pipeline operation
* Audit the pipeline: sources, transformations, flow
* Treat pipelines as knowledge‑producing machinery
* Responsibility rests with actors shaping the pipeline
