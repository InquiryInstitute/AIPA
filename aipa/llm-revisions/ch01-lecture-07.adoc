==== Conceptual Core

AI systems are participants in institutional knowledge practices. They support decisions—loan approval, hiring, content moderation, medical diagnosis. The distinction between decision support and decision delegation matters. Support means the system informs a human who retains authority; delegation means the system acts, and the human ratifies or reviews. In practice, the line is blurry. A loan officer may rubber‑stamp an algorithmic recommendation; a moderator may defer to an automated filter.

When AI becomes an institutional participant, accountability and traceability become urgent. Who is responsible when the system errs? Organizations adopt AI to improve efficiency, consistency, or scale, but adoption also reshapes the organization: roles shift, expertise is reallocated, and the locus of authority moves. From tool to actor—when AI becomes an institutional participant, it is no longer a passive instrument. It shapes what gets decided, how, and by whom.

Foucault’s insight—“the individual is an effect of power”—applies directly: the organization, the roles, the procedures are produced by the systems that structure them. AI is both constituted by institutional arrangements (who funds it, who deploys it, what policies constrain it) and constitutive of them (how work is organized, who has authority, what counts as a “decision”).

Accountability requires audit trails: who requested what, what did the system return, what action was taken, and who approved it. Governance requirements—especially in regulated domains like finance, healthcare, and employment—increasingly demand such traces. The diagram in this lecture shows a governance loop: request, AI decision, human review, action, with parallel branches to audit, model update, accountability, and policy change. The loop never closes; governance is ongoing. *We will now see how these ideas play out in concrete workflows.*

==== Technical Example

Consider loan approval. A customer applies; the system scores the application and recommends approve or deny. A loan officer reviews the recommendation and makes the final decision. Who is accountable if the loan defaults? The officer? The model? The institution? The “many‑hands” problem shows responsibility is distributed across designers, deployers, reviewers, and the organization itself.

*Many‑hands breakdown*

* **Designers** – create the scoring model and its training data.
* **Deployers** – integrate the model into the loan‑processing pipeline.
* **Reviewers** – the loan officer who validates or overrides the recommendation.
* **Organization** – sets policies, risk tolerances, and oversight mechanisms.

Content moderation offers another example. An automated filter flags posts; human moderators review a subset. The filter may produce false positives and negatives; moderators may be overworked or undertrained; policy may be ambiguous. When a wrong decision is made—content wrongly removed or wrongly allowed—who is accountable? The policy team? The model team? The moderator? The platform? Audit trails record what the system did, what the human did, and when, but accountability is not just recording—it is assignment. *Next we will reflect on the philosophical implications of this distribution of responsibility.*

===== Key Points

* Loan approval workflow: system scores, officer decides—who is accountable?
* Many‑hands distribution: designers, deployers, reviewers, organization
* Content moderation: filter + moderators + policy—accountability complex
* Audit trails: record system actions and human interventions
* Accountability: requires explicit assignment, not merely logging

==== Philosophical Reflection

Delegation of authority to non‑human actors raises deep questions. We hold humans responsible because they can give reasons, feel remorse, and change their behavior. Machines do none of these. When we delegate to a system, we are not transferring responsibility—we are distributing it. The “responsibility gap” is the worry that no one is properly accountable when an automated system causes harm. The gap is not inevitable; it is a design choice.

We can build systems with clear human‑in‑the‑loop points, audit trails, and explicit assignment of accountability. In domains where responsibility cannot be assigned—e.g., lethal autonomous weapons—delegation may be ethically impermissible. The politics of institutional AI is the politics of many hands. Organizations benefit from diffusion—no one is clearly responsible—while those harmed seek a target. Legal and regulatory frameworks are catching up: the EU AI Act, sector‑specific rules, and liability doctrines are evolving.

The designer’s task is to make accountability possible: traceable decisions, documented rationale, and clear lines of responsibility. Your knowledge graph will be used in institutional contexts. Designing for auditability from the start is the practical antidote to the responsibility gap. *Understanding the governance loop will let you embed provenance and accountability directly into the knowledge graph you will build in Lab 3.*

===== Key Points

* Responsibility gap: no one accountable when automated system harms
* Design choice: human‑in‑the‑loop, audit trails, explicit assignment
* Many hands: organizations benefit from diffusion; harmed seek target
* Legal frameworks evolving: EU AI Act, sector rules, liability doctrines
* Design for auditability: traceable decisions, documented rationale, clear responsibility
